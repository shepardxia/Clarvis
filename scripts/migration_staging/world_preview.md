## 1. [2026-02-14]

# Japanese Music Ecosystem

## Noise Tradition: Japanoise

Merzbow, founded by Masami Akita in 1979 in Japan, named after Kurt Schwitters' Dada assemblage Merzbau. Akita is the most prolific Japanoise artist, with hundreds of releases spanning decades.

The Kansai region (Osaka and Kyoto) was the crucible for Japanese noise. Kansai no wave emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s, blending punk, art-school experimentalism, industrial music, and performance art. Hijokaidan performed at extreme volumes. Yamatsuka Eye's Hanatarash was a weaponized performance art project.

### Boredoms

Boredoms formed in Osaka in 1986. Members included Yamatsuka Eye (vocals) and Yoshimi P-We (drums), drawn from the Osaka underground. Early sound: violent noise-punk / Japanoise. Pop Tatari (1992) brought international attention. Toured with Sonic Youth (1992) and Nirvana (eight consecutive shows, late 1993). Through the Super Roots series (1993-1999) they expanded toward ecstatic, repetitive music steeped in psychedelia and electronic rhythms. Vision Creation Newsun (1999) blended cut-up punk with tribal drumming, cosmic synths, and ritualistic transcendence.

### Boris

Boris formed in Tokyo in 1992, named after a Melvins track. They bridge Japanese noise culture with Western sludge, drone, and stoner metal. Genre range includes experimental rock, noise rock, drone metal, psychedelic metal, shoegaze, dream pop, J-pop, and crust punk. Drummer Atsuo was the first person in Japan to interview the Melvins. Boris has framed noise as culturally Japanese: "Noise is Japanese blues." Boris-Merzbow collaboration began in the late 1990s, spanning approximately eight releases.

### Melt-Banana

Melt-Banana formed in 1992. Noise-grind with rhythmic precision and pop instincts. Songs often clock under a minute. Became a duo in 2013: vocalist Yako and guitarist Agata.

### Les Rallizes Denudes

Les Rallizes Denudes formed in 1967 at Doshisha University, Kyoto. Core member: Takashi Mizutani (through 1996). Sound: psychedelic drone, feedback-drenched guitar, proto-shoegaze walls of sound. Released almost nothing officially -- existed primarily through bootleg recordings. On March 31, 1970, bassist Moriaki Wakabayashi orchestrated the Yodogo Incident, a Red Army hijacking of a JAL flight. Massively influential on Boris, Sunn O))), and others.

## Post-Hardcore and Screamo

### envy

envy formed in Tokyo in 1992. Absorbed American post-hardcore (Fugazi, Husker Du, Big Black) and fused it with post-rock dynamics. Debut Breathing and Dying in This Place (1996). A Dead Sinking Story (2003): fully integrated post-rock crescendo structures into hardcore, creating a template for post-rock-influenced screamo worldwide. Frontman Tetsuya Fukagawa known for "sandpaper screams." The Fallen Crimson (2020).

### Heaven in Her Arms

Heaven in Her Arms emerged from Tokyo in the mid-2000s, named after a track from Converge's Jane Doe. Part of the third wave of Japanese screamo. Both envy and Heaven in Her Arms are identified as strong influences on San Francisco's Deafheaven, whose Sunbather (2013) brought blackgaze to global attention.

### Asunojokei

Asunojokei formed in 2014 in Taito ward, Tokyo. They absorbed Deafheaven's blackgaze back into Japanese context, blending post-black metal with Dir en Grey's extremity and mathy Shimokita-kei riffs. Toured Europe in 2023 supporting Boris. This completes a feedback loop: Japanese screamo influenced American blackgaze, which influenced a new generation of Japanese post-black metal.

### Ling Tosite Sigure

Ling Tosite Sigure formed in Saitama in 2002. Mathematically precise post-hardcore drawing from shoegaze and screamo. Bridges the math rock and hardcore worlds. Male vocals by TK and female vocals by 345. They took Number Girl's technical complexity further into progressive territory.

## Number Girl

Number Girl formed in Fukuoka in August 1995 by vocalist-guitarist Shutoku Mukai. Lineup: Mukai, guitarist Hisako Tabuchi, bassist Kentaro Nakao, drummer Ahito Inazawa. Musical roots: Pixies, Sonic Youth, Husker Du. School Girl Distortional Addict (1999): major label debut. Sappukei (2000): produced by Dave Fridmann. Num-Heavymetallic (2002): incorporated dub and min'yo. Band dissolved September 2002. Reunion 2019; final show December 11, 2022. Bands citing Number Girl as inspiration: tricot, Kinoko Teikoku, Ling Tosite Sigure, Asian Kung-Fu Generation. After the breakup, Mukai formed ZAZEN BOYS with drummer Inazawa.

## Math Rock

Math rock arrived in Japan through layered transmission lines: Japan's deep jazz history and progressive rock culture (Bi Kyo Ran, founded 1973 as a King Crimson tribute band). Ruins, Tatsuya Yoshida's duo formed in 1985, primary influence was French progressive rock band Magma. Direct American connections: toe cite Ghosts and Vodka; LITE acknowledge Battles. American math rock tended toward angularity; Japanese math rock foregrounded melody, atmosphere, and melancholy.

### toe

toe formed in Tokyo in 2000. The Book About My Idle Plot on a Vague Anxiety (2005) established their template: spiraling clean guitars, punctuative rhythm sections. Drummer Kashikura Takashi is especially revered. toe founded label Machu Picchu Industrias alongside mouse on the keys, Enemies, and Tangled Hair.

### tricot

tricot formed in Kyoto in 2010. Members: vocalist-guitarist Ikkyu Nakajima, guitarist Motifour, bassist Hirohiro. Debut T H E (2013) brought math rock's technical finesse into pop territory. Motifour cited Number Girl, ACIDMAN, and Sheena Ringo as main influences.

### mouse on the keys

mouse on the keys: two former members of post-hardcore band Nine Days Wonder plus Daisuke Niitome from jazz-funk backgrounds. Blend minimalist neoclassical music with hard-hitting rock. Cite Philip Glass and Helmet as twin reference points.

## Fishmans

Fishmans formed in Minato, Tokyo in summer 1987 at Meiji Gakuin University. Started as a reggae band, evolved into ethereal psychedelic pop anchored by Shinji Sato's otherworldly falsetto. Discovered by Kazufumi Kodama of dub band Mute Beat. Producer ZAK added space rock, ambient, and shoegaze elements. Magnum opus Long Season (1996): a 35-minute single track. Shinji Sato died of heart failure on March 15, 1999, at age 33. Posthumous influence on Mac DeMarco, Cornelius, and betcover!!.

## downy and OGRE YOU ASSHOLE

downy, founded by Aoki Robin in 2000, is considered a founder of Japanese post-rock. All seven albums titled Mudai ("Untitled"). OGRE YOU ASSHOLE formed in Nagano in 2001. Influences: Talking Heads, CAN, Fugazi. Both bands operate in Tokyo's Shimokitazawa and Koenji neighborhoods.

## Regional Scenes

Tokyo Shimokitazawa: spiritual center of Japanese indie rock. Key venues: Shelter (opened 1991), Basement Bar, Three, Club Que. Shimokita-kei movement peaked late 1990s to mid-2000s. Koenji: parallel scene, Antiknock venue for metal and punk.

Osaka and Kansai: Fandango (founded 1987) for free-form improvisation. Namba Bears established by Boredoms guitarist Seiichi Yamamoto. Hokage specializes in heavy music. Osaka's smaller scale forces cross-pollination.

Kyoto: Les Rallizes Denudes emerged from student radical culture. tricot emerged decades later. Fukuoka: Number Girl demonstrated bands could come from outside Tokyo-Osaka axis.

Live House System: small independent venues, quota system ("noruma"), audiences focus solely on music.

Festival Infrastructure: Fuji Rock (since 1997, Naeba Ski Resort, 200+ bands). Summer Sonic runs in Osaka and Chiba simultaneously. Rising Sun Rock Festival (Hokkaido): exclusively Japanese bands.

## City Pop

Happy End formed in 1969. Members: Haruomi Hosono, Eiichi Ohtaki, Takashi Matsumoto, Shigeru Suzuki. Kaze Machi Roman (1971) proved Japanese-language rock viable. After disbanding (1972), Hosono formed Tin Pan Alley. In 1978, Hosono formed Yellow Magic Orchestra (YMO) with Yukihiro Takahashi and Ryuichi Sakamoto. Afrika Bambaataa sampled YMO. YMO disbanded 1984. Sakamoto: Oscar-winning film scores (The Last Emperor), died 2023.

City pop emerged late 1970s. Tatsuro Yamashita: Ride on Time (1980), For You (1982). Mariya Takeuchi's "Plastic Love" (1984) catalyzed 21st-century revival. Eiichi Ohtaki's A Long Vacation (1981). Other figures: Toshiki Kadomatsu, Anri, Miki Matsubara, Taeko Onuki. City pop's golden age coincided with Japan's asset price bubble (1986-1991).

City pop internet revival: 2017-2018 "Plastic Love" remix went viral. Primed by vaporwave (circa 2010).

## Shibuya-kei

Shibuya-kei flourished mid-to-late 1990s Tokyo. Named after HMV Shibuya record store. Flipper's Guitar (Kenji Ozawa, Keigo Oyamada) laid foundation. Cornelius produced Fantasma (1997). Pizzicato Five was most prominent internationally. Modern inheritors: cero, mitsume, mei ehara, Lamp.

## Japanese Jazz

Jazz arrived in Japan via Pacific cruise ships from 1913. Jazz kissa (listening bar): first one Black Bird opened Tokyo 1929, around 600 at peak. Three Blind Mice (founded 1970): Japanese Blue Note. East Wind (1974). DIW Records (1983, Disk Union subsidiary).

Ryo Fukui: self-taught pianist, Sapporo. Scenery (1976). Opened Slowboat jazz club 1995, died 2016. Masabumi Kikuchi (1939-2015): Poo-Sun (1970), worked with Sonny Rollins and Miles Davis. Hiromi Uehara (born 1979): met Chick Corea at 17, Berklee, Grammy winner. Hiroshi Suzuki: Cat (1975). Yasuaki Shimizu: influenced Oneohtrix Point Never.

Nujabes (Jun Seba, 1974-2010): bridged jazz and hip-hop. Samurai Champloo soundtrack. Posthumous influence on lo-fi hip-hop movement.

## Sheena Ringo and Tokyo Incidents

Sheena Ringo debuted 1998. Genre-defying: rock, jazz, chanson, traditional Japanese music. Formed Tokyo Incidents (Tokyo Jihen) in 2004. KIRINJI formed 1996 by siblings Takaki and Yasuyuki Horigome: city pop with jazz harmony and bossa nova.

## Vocaloid and Internet Music

Hatsune Miku released August 31, 2007 by Crypton Future Media. Ecosystem developed on Niconico Douga (launched December 2006). Kenshi Yonezu started as Hachi using Miku, became Japan's biggest pop star. Eve: "Kaikai Kitan" for Jujutsu Kaisen. YOASOBI: "Idol" for Oshi no Ko. ZUTOMAYO: maintained anonymity. Yoko Kanno: Cowboy Bebop soundtrack (1998) established anime soundtracks as artistically serious.

## betcover!!

betcover!! is the project of Yanase Jiro, born 1999 in Chofu, Tokyo. Started 2015 as misspelled solo bedroom project. Six-piece art rock ensemble fusing jazz-rock, progressive composition, dub, enka, and post-punk. Independent albums: Jikan (2021), Tamago (2022), Uma (2023), Yuki (2025). Tamago recorded without metronomes, mastering skipped. Yuki: Jim O'Rourke as engineer, saxophonist Kei Matsumaru. Most direct ancestor: Fishmans. Other influences: Jack White, Nick Cave, The Mars Volta, King Tubby. BCNR connection: Japan Tour 2025 special guests. 2025 world tour: 31 cities.

## Bridge Figures

Boris bridges noise, drone, shoegaze, stoner metal, ambient. envy connects post-hardcore, screamo, post-rock. Hosono threads psychedelic rock through city pop to electronic. Nujabes links jazz to hip-hop. Jim O'Rourke (American, based in Japan) connects Japanese experimental scene to international networks. Kei Matsumaru bridges Tokyo jazz, Dos Monos/black midi pipeline, and betcover!!.

## Key Connections

Kinoko Teikoku wrote "Girl Meets NUMBER GIRL." Fishmans tribute included Cornelius and Sakamoto. Asunojokei toured supporting Boris. Dos Monos-to-black midi pipeline connects to betcover!! and BCNR.

# Chinese and Taiwanese Underground Music

## The Dakou Generation

In the early 1990s, Western record companies shipped millions of unsold CDs to China as recyclable waste. These "dakou" (cut) discs had edges sawed to prevent resale but remained playable except the last track. Beijing's Xinjiekou South Street became the underground distribution hub where Nirvana, Sonic Youth, Joy Division, and the Velvet Underground entered China through customs as recycling materials. The dakou generation -- born roughly 1970-1990 -- built their aesthetic education from these damaged imports.

## Cui Jian

Known as the "Godfather of Chinese Rock." Influences: Bob Dylan, the Beatles, the Rolling Stones, Talking Heads. "Nothing to My Name" (yi wu suo you), first performed May 9, 1986, became an unofficial anthem during the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests.

## Dou Wei

Lead vocalist of Black Panther. Left 1991, formed Zuomeng. Solo: Black Dream (1994), Sunny Days (1995), Mountain River (1998). Formed E band 1999. Evolved from stadium rock to reclusive experimental composer exploring ambient, traditional Chinese folk, free jazz, spoken word. Multi-instrumentalist: electric guitars, synthesizers, guqin, dizi, ruan, drums.

## Modern Sky Records

Founded 1997 by Shen Lihui (vocalist of Sober). Grew into China's largest independent label: 300+ artists, offices in Beijing, New York, London, Berlin. Key roster: Hedgehog, Wutiaoren, New Pants. Launched Strawberry Music Festival 2010, held in 29 Chinese cities.

## D-22

Venue in Beijing's Wudaokou district, founded by Michael Pettis 2005-06. Wall Street Journal called it "the center of new music in Beijing." Launchpad for post-punk and avant-garde. Key bands: Carsick Cars, Snapline, Hedgehog. Closed January 2012.

## School Bar

Co-founded by Liu Hao (Joyside bassist) and Liu "Felix" Fei. Beijing's Gulou hutong area. Opened 2010 as D-22 shuttered.

## Key Beijing Bands

Carsick Cars: formed March 2005 by Shou Wang. Debut produced by P.K.14 frontman Yang Haisong. "Zhong Nan Hai" considered anthem of Beijing's underground. Maybe Mars label.

Re-TROS: formed Beijing 2003. Dark minimalism, motorik rhythms. Influences: Joy Division, Bauhaus. Opened for Depeche Mode on Global Spirit Tour 2017.

Snapline: side project of Carsick Cars members Li Qing and Li Weisi. Compared to The Fall.

New Pants: established 1996 under Modern Sky. Synthpop, punk rock, alternative rock. Won The Summer of Bands 2019.

Joyside: formed 2001 Beijing. Punk rock. Bassist Liu Hao co-founded School Bar.

Hedgehog: formed early 2003. Influences: Jesus and Mary Chain, Ramones, Nirvana. Evolved from garage punk toward expansive art rock. Won CMA "Band of the Year" via Summer of Bands (2019).

## Post-Rock

Wang Wen: formed 1999 in Dalian. China's most important post-rock act. Instrumental, melancholic. Echoes of Sigur Ros and Tortoise.

Zhaoze: Southern China. First post-rock band to incorporate traditional Chinese instruments. "Electric Guqin" combining ancient guqin with post-rock.

## Dialect Rock

Wutiaoren: two members from Haifeng, Guangdong. Sing in Haifeng dialect. Folky guitar and accordion. Signed Modern Sky 2014.

Jiulian Zhenren: from Lianping county, Guangdong. Sing in Hakka dialect. Members are school teachers and shop owners. Breakthrough on The Big Band (2019).

## Li Zhi

Folk-rock. Songs alluding to 1989 Tiananmen. Since April 12, 2019, all activities paused, music removed from streaming platforms. His erasure clarified political red lines.

## Taiwan

Scene emerged after martial law lifted 1987. Key venues: SCUM (1994), Underground Society, The Wall, Riverside Live House. Platforms: StreetVoice (2006), iNDIEVOX.

No Party For Cao Dong: three Golden Melody Awards 2017. Sunset Rollercoaster: dreamy city pop and psychedelic pop. Elephant Gym: bass-driven math rock trio, bridge to Japanese math rock (toe, LITE).

## Cross-Strait Dynamic

Taiwanese acts perform freely in mainland; mainland rarely tours Taiwan. Beijing's sound shaped by dakou scarcity; Taiwan developed in real-time conversation with global trends.

## The Summer of Bands (2019)

iQiyi variety show bringing indie acts to massive TV audiences. Sparked renewed interest in rock among young Chinese audiences.

## 3. [2026-02-14]

# The Windmill Scene

## The Windmill Venue

150-capacity pub in Brixton, South London, built 1971. Purchased by Seamus McCausland 1992. Tim Perry began booking experimental acts 2002. No stage barriers, shared performer-audience space.

## Dan Carey and Speedy Wunderground

Dan Carey, producer based in South London. Co-founded Speedy Wunderground 2013 with Alexis Smith and Pierre Hall. Recording manifesto: every track recorded in a single day, one overdub per player. Signature sound: live, urgent, rough edges intact. Released early singles for BCNR ("Athens, France" 2019) and Squid (Town Centre EP 2019).

## black midi (2017-2024)

Formed at London's BRIT School 2016. Members: Geordie Greep, Matt Kwasniewski-Kelvin, Morgan Simpson, Cameron Picton. Sound: maximalist collision of math rock, progressive rock, post-punk, avant-jazz. Influences: King Crimson, Captain Beefheart, Frank Zappa, Can.

Albums: Schlagenheim (2019, produced by Dan Carey), Cavalcade (2021), Hellfire (2022). Hiatus August 2024. Greep released solo album The New Sound. Kwasniewski-Kelvin passed away January 2026 at age 26.

## Black Country, New Road (2018-present)

Formed Cambridge 2018. Members: Tyler Hyde, Lewis Evans, Georgia Ellery, May Kershaw, Charlie Wayne, Isaac Wood, Luke Mark. Sprawling compositions blending post-punk, post-rock, klezmer, free jazz, chamber pop.

Ants from Up There (2022, Ninja Tune) -- Metacritic 92/100. Isaac Wood departed January 31, 2022. Remaining members redistributed vocals into democratic ensemble.

## Squid (2016-present)

Formed Brighton 2016. Propulsive post-punk infusing jazz, indie rock, prog, krautrock. Cites Neu! and This Heat. Albums: Bright Green Field (2021, Warp), O Monolith (2023), Cowards (2025). Most stable trajectory of core trio.

## Extended Acts

shame: Wire-inflected post-punk. Sorry: angular guitar pop. Dry Cleaning: Florence Shaw's spoken-word vocals. The Orchestra (For Now): maximalist "London prog," next-generation Windmill act. Maruja: Manchester, explosive post-rock/punk/jazz with brass. Geese: Brooklyn parallel.

## Windmill Sound Characteristics

Rhythmic complexity, dynamic extremes, genre promiscuity, literary/conceptual lyrics, live urgency. Influenced by Slint, This Heat, Wire, King Crimson, Captain Beefheart.

## Latin American Extensions

Geordie Greep's The New Sound (2024): bossa nova, MPB, samba influences, recorded in London and Sao Paulo. Candelabro (Santiago, Chile): influenced by black midi, Squid, BCNR. unperro andaluz (Mexico City). Todos Mis Amigos Estan Tristes (Temuco, Chile).

## Global Influence

SENTRIES (Lethbridge, Alberta), Racing Mount Pleasant (Ann Arbor), Weatherday (Sweden), Sprain (Los Angeles).

## betcover!! and Japan-UK Pipeline

Dos Monos opened for black midi at UNIT Tokyo (September 5, 2019), leading to joint European tour and creative exchange. BCNR announced betcover!! as special guests for Japan Tour 2025. Kei Matsumaru connects Dos Monos, black midi, Eiko Ishibashi, Jim O'Rourke, and betcover!!. Pattern: Japanese experimental acts open for Windmill bands in Tokyo, mutual interest develops, European touring follows.

## 4. [2026-02-14]

# Heavy Music: Metal, Mathcore, and the Extreme

## Metalcore

Metalcore emerged in the late 1980s from crossover between hardcore punk and thrash metal. Cro-Mags and Agnostic Front incorporated thrash riffs; Suicidal Tendencies adopted punk attitudes.

### Converge
Formed 1990, Salem, Massachusetts. Vocalist Jacob Bannon, guitarist Kurt Ballou. Key album: *Jane Doe* (2001, Equal Vision Records) -- definitive mathcore statement combining technical intricacy with visceral emotional impact. Earlier albums pushed hardcore toward metallic riffing and complex structures. *When Forever Comes Crashing* (1998) revealed the mathcore blueprint.

Bannon's visual art and design work through Deathwish Inc. established a visual language as influential as the music. Converge is the single most important bridge between hardcore punk and metal. AllMusic describes them as combining "the aggression of hardcore and metal with the complexity and polyrhythms of jazz."

Later discography: *You Fail Me* (2004) added sludge/doom sections. *Axe to Fall* (2009) expanded sonic palette. *The Dusk in Us* (2017) explored atmospheric impulses. *Bloodmoon: I* (2021) -- collaboration with Chelsea Wolfe.

Influenced: Architects (UK), Parkway Drive, Heaven in Her Arms (named after a Converge song), the broader mathcore/metalcore/post-hardcore ecosystem.

### GodCity Studio / Kurt Ballou
Kurt Ballou's GodCity Studio, Salem, Massachusetts. Production philosophy: dynamics, room ambience, guitars that sound dangerous, physical drum presence. Clarity without sterility, aggression without muddiness.

Notable recordings: Nails (powerviolence), High on Fire (*Death Is This Communion*), Torche (sludge pop), Code Orange (nu-metalcore), Kvelertak (Norwegian rock-metal). Ballou could make doom massive, grindcore articulate, hardcore three-dimensional. GodCity became a genre signifier for uncompromising, present, raw-but-not-lo-fi production.

### Melodic Metalcore
Killswitch Engage merged metalcore with melodic death metal -- clean singing, harmonized guitar leads, anthemic choruses alongside breakdowns and screamed vocals. Lineage includes As I Lay Dying, August Burns Red.

### Parkway Drive
Formed 2003, Byron Bay, Australia. Geographic isolation from established metal scenes produced metalcore that was physically powerful but sun-bleached, more primal energy than technical showmanship. Became Australia's most successful metal export. Opened paths for Polaris, Northlane, The Amity Affliction, Alpha Wolf, Thornhill. Australian metalcore: serrated, beatdown-heavy rhythms with melodic edges.

### Meshuggah and Djent
Meshuggah, formed 1987, Sweden. Guitarist Fredrik Thordendal coined "djent" (onomatopoeic for their percussive palm-muted guitar tone). Pioneered polymetric riffing -- odd groupings against steady 4/4 pulse creating illusion of shifting time signatures while maintaining groove. Key albums: *Destroy Erase Improve* (1995), *Chaosphere* (1998), *Nothing* (2002), *obZen* (2008). Djent is about groove and syncopation; mathcore is about destabilization.

Djent movement emerged late 2000s through bedroom producers and online communities. One of the first metal movements shaped by internet culture rather than geographic scenes.

**Periphery** -- founded by Misha Mansoor, self-titled debut 2010. Complex polyrhythms with pop-influenced hooks.
**TesseracT** -- ambient and post-rock sensibilities applied to djent. Acle Kahney learned techniques via Meshuggah's online forum.
**Animals as Leaders** -- Tosin Abasi fused djent with jazz fusion and classical guitar technique.

### Progressive Metalcore
ERRA, Northlane, Currents -- ambient textures, clean vocals, djent-influenced production, technical guitar work. Spiritbox represents current evolution, incorporating metalcore, nu-metal, prog, shoegaze, djent. Vocalist Courtney LaPlante cites Deftones (*White Pony*), TesseracT, Nine Inch Nails as key influences.

### Deathcore
Fuses death metal extremity (blast beats, guttural vocals, tremolo picking) with metalcore structures (breakdowns, verse-chorus). Emerged early 2000s through Suicide Silence, Whitechapel, Despised Icon.

Lorna Shore's "To the Hellfire" went viral 2021, combining symphonic orchestration with extreme heaviness, reaching mainstream audiences. Brand of Sacrifice, Slaughter to Prevail, Shadow of Intent benefited from renewed interest.

Knocked Loose -- metallic hardcore with crossover appeal, represents the hardcore side of the 2020s heavy music revival.

## Mathcore

Volatile hybrid of hardcore punk aggression, heavy metal physicality, and math rock rhythmic complexity. Embraces dissonance, atonality, jarring time signature changes. Combines math rock (Don Caballero, Slint) with hardcore, adding distortion, screaming, and blast beats.

### The Dillinger Escape Plan
Formed 1997, Morris Plains, New Jersey. Technical apex of mathcore.

*Calculating Infinity* (1999, Relapse Records) -- vocalist Dimitri Minakakis, guitarist Ben Weinman, drummer Chris Pennie. Blast beats stopping on a dime, impossible guitar lines, careening between grindcore speed and eerie silence. Pennie's drumming was inhuman.

Greg Puciato replaced Minakakis, expanding vocal range from guttural screams to melodic singing. *Miss Machine* (2004) added electronics. *Ire Works* (2007) incorporated Mike Patton-esque experimentation. *Dissociation* (2016) was their planned final album. Final show December 29, 2017, Terminal 5, New York.

Live performances were infamous -- Ben Weinman destroyed equipment, climbed structures, threw himself into crowds while playing.

### Botch
Formed 1993, Tacoma, Washington. Guitarist Dave Knudson, vocalist Dave Verellen. *We Are the Romans* (1999, Hydra Head Industries) -- angular, dissonant hardcore, intellectually rigorous and physically punishing. *An Anthology of Dead Ends* (2002). Broke up 2002. Knudson went on to Minus the Bear, retaining structural sophistication in indie rock context.

### Mathcore's Emergence (Late 1990s)
Converging factors: hardcore diversifying (Integrity, Earth Crisis drawing from metal; Kiss It Goodbye, Deadguy from noise rock; Fugazi, Drive Like Jehu from post-hardcore). Math rock influence from Don Caballero, Slint, Rodan. King Crimson's *Discipline* (1981) provided foundational template of interlocking polyrhythmic guitar patterns. Death metal/grindcore bands exploring rhythmic complexity (Gorguts, early Meshuggah).

DIY label infrastructure: Hydra Head Industries, Relapse Records, Level Plane, Deathwish Inc., Translation Loss.

Coalesce was among the founding mathcore acts alongside Converge, Dillinger, and Botch.

### Car Bomb
Formed 2000, Long Island, New York. Took Meshuggah's djent influences and Dillinger's chaos, added studio manipulation, noise, rhythmic displacement. *w^w^^w^w* (2016, Relapse).

### Frontierer
UK/US collaboration. Self-titled debut 2015, *Unloved* (2018). Cybergrind meets mathcore -- programmed drums, guitar tones manipulated into synthesizer textures.

### Every Time I Die
Formed 1998, Buffalo, New York. Mathcore's more accessible side. Vocalist Keith Buckley (witty, literary lyrics), guitarists Jordan Buckley and Andy Williams incorporated Southern rock influences. *Last Night in Town* (2001), *Gutter Phenomenon* (2005). Broke up late 2021.

### Japanese Mathcore/Screamo Connection
Heaven in Her Arms -- named after a Converge song from *When Forever Comes Crashing*. Atmospheric, emotionally intense post-hardcore with screamed vocals.

envy -- formed 1992, Tokyo. Blended post-rock expansiveness with hardcore intensity. Absorbed American post-hardcore (Fugazi, Husker Du, Big Black). *From Here To Eternity* (1998) is pure Japanese screamo; *A Dead Sinking Story* (2003) fully integrated post-rock crescendo structures.

Japanese screamo wave of the 2000s: Killie, Funeral Diner, Heaven in Her Arms -- took mathcore's emotional intensity, married it to post-rock's patience.

## Technical Death Metal

Death metal emerged mid-1980s: Possessed, Death, Morbid Angel pushed thrash toward greater extremity -- lower tunings, blast beats, guttural vocals, chromatic riffing.

### Three Waves
**First wave (early 1990s):** Death (from *Human* onward), Atheist, Cynic, Pestilence fused death metal with jazz harmony and progressive structures.

**Second wave (mid-1990s):** Gorguts, Cryptopsy, Suffocation emphasized dissonance and rhythmic brutality. Gorguts' *Obscura* (1998) -- landmark of dissonant, avant-garde death metal. Angular, almost atonal. Influenced an entire generation willing to prioritize atmosphere and strangeness over traditional death metal conventions.

**Third wave (2000s-present):** Catalyzed by Necrophagist (Germany), whose *Epitaph* (2004) redefined the genre's technical ceiling. Inspired Beyond Creation, Allegaeon, The Faceless, Archspire.

### Archspire
Canadian band. Current apex of technical death metal. Drew from Origin, Spawn of Possession, Necrophagist, Obscura. Vocals, guitars, bass, and drums all operating at the edge of human capability.

### Dissonant Branch
**Deathspell Omega** (France) -- trilogy: *Si Monumentum Requires, Circumspice*; *Fas -- Ite, Maledicti, in Ignem Aeternum*; *Paracletus*. Combined black metal tremolo picking with death metal brutality and extreme harmonic/rhythmic complexity.

**Ulcerate** (New Zealand) -- death metal that feels geological: massive, oppressive, disorienting. Incorporates doom and sludge influences. Sustained atmospheric heaviness over individual riffs.

## Post-Black Metal and Blackgaze

Post-black metal retained black metal's sonic techniques (tremolo-picked guitars, blast beats, shrieked vocals) while inverting ideological charge -- replacing nihilism with transcendence, darkness with beauty, misanthropy with melancholy.

### Norwegian Black Metal Foundation
Early 1990s Norwegian second wave: Mayhem, Darkthrone, Burzum, Immortal, Emperor. Euronymous (Oystein Aarseth), Mayhem's guitarist, opened record shop Helvete ("Hell") in Oslo, 1991 -- informal scene headquarters. Established extremity as authenticity marker, transgression as aesthetic principle.

### Alcest
Led by Stephane "Neige" Paut (born 1985, France). Invented blackgaze. *Le Secret* EP (2005) mixed shoegaze textures with black metal tremolo guitars and screamed vocals. *Souvenirs d'un autre monde* (2007, Prophecy Productions) combined Burzum's tremolo wall-of-sound with Slowdive's reverb-drenched textures. Used black metal's intensity to convey wonder and light rather than nihilism and darkness.

### Deafheaven
Formed February 2010, San Francisco. Vocalist George Clarke, guitarist Kerry McCoy.

*Roads to Judah* (2011, Deathwish Inc.) -- black metal ferocity through shoegaze texture and post-rock patience. *Sunbather* (2013, Deathwish Inc.) -- soft pink cover with modernist sans-serif typography, deliberate indie rock crossover. Embraced by Pitchfork, NPR. Triggered intense backlash from black metal purists who labeled them frauds. *Infinite Granite* (2021) leaned fully into shoegaze, reducing screamed vocals. *Lonely People with Power* (2025) returned to heavier terrain.

Influenced by envy and Heaven in Her Arms (Japanese screamo-to-blackgaze pipeline).

### Liturgy
Led by Haela Hunt-Hendrix, Brooklyn. "Transcendental Black Metal" manifesto presented 2009 at Black Metal Theory symposium -- proposed recasting black metal's nihilism as transcendent and sacred.

*Renihilation* (2009). *Aesthethica* (2011, Thrill Jockey). *The Ark Work* (2015) incorporated glockenspiels, bagpipes, strings, ritual chanting, MIDI horns. Post-black metal's most philosophically ambitious project.

### Japanese Screamo-to-Blackgaze Pipeline
Distinct from French blackgaze lineage. Japanese bands trace through envy's post-hardcore DNA rather than shoegaze. Both arrive at similar sonic territory (tremolo guitars, atmospheric washes, emotional intensity) but Japanese version retains more of screamo's jagged urgency, less of shoegaze's narcotic drift.

**envy** (Tokyo, 1992) -- pioneered hardcore intensity with post-rock expansiveness, incorporating tremolo-picked guitars and crescendo structures that became blackgaze signatures. Foundational influence on blackgaze broadly.

**Heaven in Her Arms** -- combined post-rock, hardcore, metal, early 90s screamo. Treads line between post-metal and black metal. Strong influence on Deafheaven.

**Asunojokei** (Tokyo, 2014) -- third generation. Screamo-infused post-black/post-rock/experimental metal. Influenced by black metal and Japanese pop culture. Supported Boris on 2023 European tour.

Feedback loop: Japanese screamo influenced American blackgaze (Deafheaven), which influenced new Japanese post-black metal (Asunojokei).

### Shoegaze and Black Metal: Shared DNA
Both prioritize texture over riff, atmosphere over clarity, wash of sound over individual voices. Shoegaze emerged late-1980s Britain. My Bloody Valentine's Kevin Shields pioneered "glide guitar" -- strumming chords while manipulating tremolo arm for pitch warps, achieving wall of sound without chorus or flanger pedals. Black metal's tremolo picking creates similar sustained, shimmering walls of distorted guitar. Both use distortion as texture generator, favor repetition and duration, privilege mood over narrative.

### Atmospheric Black Metal vs. Post-Black Metal
Atmospheric black metal: heavy incorporation of atmospheric/dreamy textures, less aggressive, common keyboard use, slower to mid-tempo pacing. Post-black metal: draws equally from post-rock and/or sludge metal, indicates departure from black metal's ideological orthodoxy.

**Wolves in the Throne Room** and **Agalloch** -- Pacific Northwest. Combined melodic and raw atmospheric black metal with post-rock and post-metal. Nature-focused thematics connected to American wilderness landscapes.

**Oranssi Pazuzu** -- Finland, psychedelic black metal.
**Blut Aus Nord** -- France, industrial atmospheres.

## Post-Metal and Sludge

### Sludge Metal Origins
California hardcore punk bands early-to-mid-1980s (Black Flag, Flipper, Fang) slowed tempos and embraced Black Sabbath's influence. The Melvins expanded this approach, catalyzing both the Seattle grunge scene and the Louisiana sludge scene (Eyehategod, Crowbar, Acid Bath).

### Neurosis
Pivotal post-metal band. Started as hardcore punk outfit 1985. *Through Silver in Blood* (1996) effectively invented post-metal: crushing heaviness interspersed with dark ambient passages, tribal percussion, cinematic scope.

### Isis
Formed 1997. *Oceanic* (2002) fused post-rock atmospherics with metal and hardcore, defined post-metal for the 2000s. Lineage continues through Cult of Luna, Russian Circles, Pelican.

### Russian Circles
Instrumental post-metal -- no vocals, pure architecture of tension, release, crescendo, collapse.

### We Lost The Sea
Sydney, Australia. After vocalist Chris Torpy's death by suicide in 2013, transformed into instrumental ensemble. *Departure Songs* (2015) -- double album featuring high school girls' choir, cello, trumpet. Narrative-driven post-metal telling stories of historical events like the Challenger disaster.

## Bridge Artists and Cross-Pollination

### Boris
Formed 1992, Tokyo. Spans sludge metal, drone, noise, psychedelia, minimalism, experimental rock, noise rock, shoegaze, dream pop, J-pop, crust punk. Drummer Atsuo was first person in Japan to interview the Melvins. Boris and Merzbow (Masami Akita) have collaborated since 1997 (~eight releases), semi-improvisational process. Bridges hardcore punk and metal. *Jane Doe* (2001) introduced experimental approaches and rhythmic complexity that became defining features of metalcore, mathcore, and post-hardcore simultaneously.

# Jazz and Hip-Hop

## Bebop and Modal Jazz

Charlie Parker's bebop (1940s) established jazz as virtuosic small-group improvisation over rapid harmonic changes. Emphasis on individual voice and technical mastery prefigured hip-hop MC culture.

Bill Evans brought pianistic lyricism and harmonic subtlety. His work with Miles Davis on *Kind of Blue* (1959) shifted jazz from chord-based to scale-based modal improvisation.

Miles Davis is the central pivot point in jazz's evolution. Moved through bebop, cool jazz, modal, fusion, and electric abstraction. His ethos of constant reinvention became foundational to hip-hop's creative evolution model.

## Fusion

Miles Davis's electric period: *In a Silent Way* (1969), *Bitches Brew* (1970), *Agharta* (1975). *Bitches Brew* abandoned swing for rock backbeats and electric bass grooves with electronically processed trumpet.

Davis's electric band alumni spawned fusion projects: Tony Williams formed Lifetime. John McLaughlin's Mahavishnu Orchestra. Chick Corea's Return to Forever. Wayne Shorter and Joe Zawinul formed Weather Report (lush, textural fusion anticipating ambient music). Herbie Hancock's Headhunters released *Head Hunters* (1973), adding funk to fusion.

Fusion made jazz louder, groove-oriented, and amplified — this sonic transformation was crucial when hip-hop producers later dug through vinyl bins for source material.

## Japanese Jazz

### Jazz Kissa Culture

Jazz arrived in Japan via transpacific ocean liners as early as the 1910s. First jazz kissa (jazz cafe): Black Bird, Tokyo, 1929. By 1935, around 80 jazz kissa operated across Japan. WWII interrupted (jazz classified as "enemy music"), but post-war American occupation revived the scene — US military bases employed Japanese musicians. At peak, around 600 jazz kissa operated across Japan.

Jazz kissa philosophy: recorded sound deserves the same seriousness as live performance. Dark, vinyl-stacked rooms with vintage American audio gear. Some banned conversation entirely. Deep listening as cultural practice.

### 1970s Japanese Jazz Golden Age

Toshiko Akiyoshi drew on Japanese culture in big band compositions co-led with Lew Tabackin, incorporating traditional instruments like the tsuzumi.

Key independent labels:
- **Three Blind Mice** — founded by Takeshi Fujii, 1970. Engineer Yoshihiko Kannari recorded most releases with intimate mic setups. Over 130 albums. The Japanese Blue Note.
- **East Wind** — established 1974. Promoted Japanese scene while giving access to American artists (Art Farmer, Sam Jones).
- **DIW Records** — founded 1983. Bridged Japanese and American avant-garde. ~300 albums including Art Ensemble of Chicago, David Murray, John Zorn.

### Ryo Fukui

Self-taught pianist from Sapporo, far from Tokyo's music industry. Began playing at age 22, absorbing Bill Evans, Miles Davis, John Coltrane. *Scenery* (1976) fuses modal, bop, and cool jazz with warmth and melodic sensibility. Follow-up: *Mellow Dream* (1977). Opened the Slowboat jazz club in Sapporo with wife Yasuko in 1995. Died 2016. Posthumous internet rediscovery via YouTube algorithms — *Scenery* accumulated over 12 million views, introduced to audiences via jazz, instrumental hip-hop, and lo-fi beat listeners.

### Hiromi Uehara

Born 1979, Hamamatsu. Started piano at age 6. Met Chick Corea at 17 in Tokyo; he invited her to play at his concert the next day. Attended Berklee from 1999, signed to Telarc before graduation. Blends stride, post-bop, progressive rock, classical, nu jazz, and fusion. Grammy winner (Best Contemporary Jazz Album). Performed at 2020 Tokyo Olympics opening ceremony.

## Jazz-Rap Pipeline

### Foundational Sampling

Early hip-hop DJs needed breakbeats; jazz recordings provided them. Key architects:

**DJ Premier** (Gang Starr) — chopped jazz samples into staccato loops, reversed/pitch-shifted horn hits for melodic hooks. Key albums: *Daily Operation* (1992), *Hard to Earn* (1994). Gritty drums, noir-ish horn samples, Guru's deadpan delivery.

**Pete Rock** — smoother, more melodic productions emphasizing trumpet and saxophone samples. *Mecca and the Soul Brother* (1992) with CL Smooth.

**Guru** — *Jazzmatazz* series (4 volumes, 1993-2007). Rapped over live instrumentation from Donald Byrd, Lonnie Liston Smith, Roy Ayers, Branford Marsalis. Direct hybrid of jazz and hip-hop.

### A Tribe Called Quest

Formed Queens, 1985. Core: Q-Tip, Phife Dawg, DJ Ali Shaheed Muhammad. Central figures in Native Tongues collective alongside De La Soul, Jungle Brothers, Queen Latifah. Native Tongues offered a third path: Afrocentric, playful, intellectually curious, sonically adventurous.

Q-Tip's production rooted in crate-digging. *The Low End Theory* (1991) — named after its bass-heavy mix (engineered with Bob Power). Sampled Art Blakey, Ron Carter, Weather Report, Cannonball Adderley. Ron Carter recorded original basslines for several tracks — collaboration across generations, not just pastiche. "Verses from the Abstract" sampled Carter's "Foosh" from *Uptown Conversation* (1970).

Discography: *People's Instinctive Travels* (1990), *The Low End Theory* (1991), *Midnight Marauders* (1993), *Beats, Rhymes and Life* (1996), *The Love Movement* (1998). Reunion album *We Got It from Here... Thank You 4 Your Service* (2016) debuted #1 on Billboard 200. Phife Dawg died March 22, 2016 from diabetes complications.

## Nujabes and Lo-Fi Hip-Hop

### Nujabes

Jun Seba (Nujabes = name reversed), Japanese producer/DJ from Shibuya. Co-owned Guinness Records. Approached production from a jazz listener's perspective. Label: Hydeout Productions.

*Metaphorical Music* (2003) — "Feather," "Lady Brown." Piano loops, brushed drums, subtly processed samples. *Modal Soul* (2005) — "Luv(sic) Part 3" featuring Shing02.

Composed music for *Samurai Champloo* (2004, dir. Shinichiro Watanabe), co-produced with Fat Jon and Force of Nature. Anime blending Edo-period samurai culture with hip-hop aesthetics. Tracks "Battlecry" and "Aruarian Dance" became iconic; introduced Nujabes to global audience.

Died February 26, 2010 in a traffic accident in Tokyo, age 36. His music became foundational to the lo-fi hip-hop / "study beats" movement.

### J Dilla

James Yancey, Detroit producer. Revolutionized beatmaking with "drunk" or "off-grid" drumming — hi-hats slightly ahead/behind, kicks in unexpected places, creating human feel in sample-based music. Worked with Slum Village, The Pharcyde. Solo instrumental album *Donuts* (2006) released three days before his death from lupus.

Lo-fi hip-hop movement (2010s YouTube explosion, "Lofi Girl" 24/7 streams) is essentially the child of Nujabes and J Dilla. Aesthetic: vinyl crackle, jazz piano/guitar samples, off-grid drums, melancholic mood. Hip-hop as ambient/functional music.

## Kendrick Lamar and Jazz in Mainstream Hip-Hop

*To Pimp a Butterfly* (2015) — 78-minute jazz-inflected meditation on Blackness, fame, and America. Followed *good kid, m.A.A.d city* (2012, West Coast G-funk).

Core musicians:
- **Kamasi Washington** (tenor sax) — spiritual jazz intensity, Coltrane-reminiscent. Played on "u" and "How Much a Dollar Cost." His own *The Epic* arrived later in 2015.
- **Thundercat** (Stephen Bruner, bass) — virtuosic playing on "Wesley's Theory" and "These Walls."
- **Terrace Martin** (sax, keys, production) — deep knowledge of jazz history and West Coast traditions. Worked with Kendrick since *good kid, m.A.A.d city*.

Jazz as structure, not texture: "For Free? (Interlude)" — double-time rapping over bebop piano vamp, flow mimicking jazz soloist phrasing. "u" collapses into chaotic dissonance mirroring psychological breakdown. "Mortal Man" ends with posthumous Tupac "interview."

Won five Grammys including Best Rap Album. Awarded Pulitzer Prize for Music (2018) — first non-classical/jazz work to receive the honor.

## MF DOOM and Abstract Hip-Hop

Daniel Dumile. Originally Zev Love X of KMD with brother DJ Subroc. KMD's *Black Bastards* completed 1993, shelved by Elektra. Subroc killed by a car weeks before the cancellation. Dumile disappeared, spent years homeless.

Re-emerged 1997 as MF DOOM — supervillain character from Marvel's Doctor Doom, metal mask. *Operation: Doomsday* (1999) on Fondle 'Em Records. Lo-fi, sample-heavy, pulling from Sesame Street, 1960s soul, cartoon themes.

*Madvillainy* (2004) — collaboration with Madlib as Madvillain. Madlib's production: fragmented, jazz-inflected, surreal — incomplete loops, unpredictable swing, dissolving samples. DOOM's rhymes: dense internal rhymes, offbeat rhythms, layered pop culture references. Tracks: "Meat Grinder," "Accordion," "All Caps." Proof that hip-hop could be as abstract as free jazz.

Died October 31, 2020 (revealed two months later).

## UK Modern Jazz — South London Scene

Jazz renaissance centered in Brixton, Peckham, and surrounding South London neighborhoods.

**Shabaka Hutchings** — figurehead, leading three projects: Sons of Kemet (sax, tuba, two drummers — African/Caribbean folkloric sources), The Comet Is Coming (psychedelic electronic jazz-funk), Shabaka and the Ancestors (South African-inflected spiritual jazz). Drew from multiple diasporic traditions simultaneously.

**Yussef Dayes** — expressive drummer, first appeared in United Vibrations. Formed Yussef Kamaal with Kamaal Williams (2015). Album *Black Focus* (2016) — cosmic-jazz synths and breakbeats; jazz that functions on dance floors.

*We Out Here* compilation — curated by Hutchings, recorded over three days in August 2017. Nine tracks presenting the UK jazz vanguard.

Scene absorbs grime, broken beat, and club culture alongside African and Caribbean diasporic roots. These musicians grew up with Dilla and Madlib — hip-hop's influence on groove and rhythm is explicit.

## Bridge Artists

**mouse on the keys** — jazz-math rock nexus. Two former members of Nine Days Wonder (post-hardcore/post-rock) plus Daisuke Niitome from jazz-funk/hip-hop backgrounds. Blend minimalist neoclassical with hard-hitting rock. Cite Philip Glass and Helmet as twin references. Hardcore musicians picking up jazz/classical instruments while retaining rhythmic intensity.

**betcover!!** — jazz, prog, and experimental J-pop convergence. Jazz harmony, complex time signatures, and improvisational approaches within pop structures. Treats jazz as compositional toolkit rather than discrete genre.

**Tyler, the Creator** — emerged 2009 as Odd Future ringleader. Early work dark, lo-fi, influenced by Pharrell, MF DOOM, early Eminem. *Flower Boy* (2017) incorporated neo-soul and jazz-funk textures. *Call Me If You Get Lost* (2021) sampled Alvin Cash, Brother Jack McDuff, Hot Chocolate Band — jazz-funk sources in maximalist productions. "Lemonhead" and "Sweet / I Thought You Wanted to Dance" update Native Tongues' eclectic sampling for a new generation.

# Rock, Post-Punk, and Shoegaze

## Progressive Rock

Progressive rock emerged late 1960s. Musicians with classical and jazz training treated rock as a compositional medium.

King Crimson released In the Court of the Crimson King (1969), a founding statement of progressive rock. Extended compositions, dynamic range, mellotron, woodwinds, irregular meters. Robert Fripp's Discipline-era lineup (1981-84) introduced interlocking polyrhythmic guitar patterns and gamelan-influenced structures that became foundational to math rock. Don Caballero drew directly from Discipline's angular precision.

Canterbury scene (Soft Machine, Caravan) leaned toward jazz. Symphonic branch (Yes, Genesis, ELP) pursued orchestral grandeur.

Art rock overlaps with prog but emphasizes conceptual ambition over technical virtuosity. Talking Heads, Roxy Music, and Radiohead occupy this space. Art rock's willingness to incorporate minimalism, repetition, and non-Western structures made it a more direct ancestor of post-punk and post-rock than symphonic prog.

Radiohead evolved from guitar rock (The Bends, 1995) through art-rock ambition (OK Computer, 1997) to electronic texture (Kid A, 2000). Demonstrated that rock musicians could reinvent themselves completely. Influenced underground music's adventurous acts broadly.

Prog's philosophical claim: rock instrumentation could serve purposes beyond verse-chorus-verse. Texture, duration, and atmosphere as valid compositional goals. This echoes through post-rock, math rock, and every band using rock instruments for non-rock purposes.

## Krautrock

Krautrock developed in West Germany, late 1960s-early 1970s. Drew from Karlheinz Stockhausen's electronic music, minimalist repetition, and deliberate rejection of Anglo-American blues-rock conventions. Parallel evolution to British prog but with fundamentally different DNA.

### CAN

Members Holger Czukay and Irmin Schmidt studied under Stockhausen. Built sound around improvisation, tape manipulation, and motorik rhythms. Jaki Liebezeit made the motorik beat iconic. Vocalist Damo Suzuki sang in a melange of Japanese, English, and German via "instant composing."

CAN influenced David Bowie, Talking Heads, Joy Division, Jesus and Mary Chain, Radiohead, and PIL. Key album: Tago Mago (1971).

Direct lineage to Windmill scene: in May 2018, black midi performed an improvised set with Damo Suzuki at the Windmill, Brixton. Cassette released September 2018. Cameron Picton confirmed that live jam was raw material for several tracks on Schlagenheim -- the band zeroed in on compelling bits from the tape, deconstructed and reassembled them into songs.

### Kraftwerk

Pioneered pop music performed exclusively on synthesizers. Called their approach "robot pop." Initially connected to krautrock, transitioned to pure electronic sound.

Krautrock splits into two downstream channels: electronic branch (Kraftwerk to synth-pop to techno) and rhythmic/textural branch (CAN and Neu! to post-punk, post-rock, experimental rock).

### Neu!

Formed by Klaus Dinger and Michael Rother after leaving Kraftwerk. Refined the motorik beat into its purest form. Relentless 4/4 rhythm, minimal melodic variation, hypnotic repetition creating trance states. Influenced post-punk, shoegaze, and post-rock -- the idea that a single sustained groove could be an entire composition's foundation.

## Punk to Post-Punk

Punk erupted mid-1970s. Ramones, Sex Pistols, Patti Smith. Stripped rock to basics, demonstrating anyone could participate. The democratization of music-making proved more consequential than punk's specific sound.

### Joy Division

Formed in Salford in 1976 as Warsaw, renamed Joy Division early 1978. Took punk's stripped-back approach, infused it with dread, space, and mechanical rhythms. Unknown Pleasures (1979) invented post-punk's emotional palette -- simultaneously skeletal and overwhelming.

Joy Division's influence splintered into at least six evolutionary paths:
- Shoegaze: Joy Division to noise + dream pop to My Bloody Valentine to Japanese shoegaze to Kinoko Teikoku
- Japanese post-hardcore: Fugazi to envy to Heaven in Her Arms to Asunojokei
- Windmill scene: post-punk DNA to black midi, BCNR, Squid, Maruja
- Chinese post-punk: Joy Division to Re-TROS, New Pants, Hedgehog, Beijing scene

### Post-Punk

Post-punk emerged in punk's immediate wake. Artists galvanized by punk's energy incorporated electronic music, funk, dub, art rock, and avant-garde.

Gang of Four (Leeds) incorporated funk rhythms and Marxist politics into angular, percussive guitar. Entertainment! (1979) influenced decades of art-punk and dance-punk. Public Image Ltd (John Lydon post-Sex Pistols) experimented with dub and dissonance. The Fall pursued relentless repetition with Mark E. Smith's caustic wordplay. Siouxsie and the Banshees moved toward gothic atmospherics. Wire compressed post-punk into taut, minimal bursts.

### Chinese Post-Punk

Western music reached Chinese youth through "dakou" (cut-out) discs -- surplus CDs and cassettes shipped to China for recycling, sold illegally from 1993 onward. Joy Division, Bauhaus, the Ramones reached Beijing through this accidental distribution network.

Re-TROS (Rebuilding the Rights of Statues) took direct influence from Joy Division and Bauhaus. New Pants became pioneers of new wave in China, blending synthpop, punk, and rock. Hedgehog describe melodies rooted in old Chinese music with style influenced by modern rock.

D-22, opened in Beijing in 2006 by former Wall Street banker Michael Pettis, became the center of new music in Beijing and home to the city's counterculture.

## Hardcore to Post-Hardcore

Hardcore punk (Black Flag, Minor Threat, Bad Brains) accelerated punk's tempo and aggression in early 1980s. Post-hardcore emerged when hardcore musicians incorporated melodic, experimental, and atmospheric elements without abandoning intensity.

### Fugazi

Formed in Washington D.C. in 1986 by Ian MacKaye (Minor Threat) and Joe Lally. Guy Picciotto and Brendan Canty joined 1987. Embraced reggae, dub, funk, art rock, and post-punk while maintaining hardcore's ethical framework (all-ages shows, independent releases, anti-corporate stance). Demonstrated hardcore could be rhythmically sophisticated and dynamically varied.

At the Drive-In cited Fugazi as a major influence. Relationship of Command (2000) became a post-hardcore landmark. When ATDI split, Cedric Bixler-Zavala and Omar Rodriguez-Lopez formed The Mars Volta, pursuing prog-punk. Uncut described them: "Imagine a jam session between King Crimson, Fugazi and '70s Miles."

### Japanese Post-Hardcore

Envy, formed Tokyo 1992, combined breathless hardcore with melodic alt-rock and screamo's loud-soft dynamic. Progressively incorporated post-rock elements -- soaring, cinematic passages building and releasing over extended durations. More overtly cathartic than American counterpart, willing to be beautiful in ways Western hardcore avoids. Spawned a subgenre of post-rock-influenced screamo.

Heaven in Her Arms (named after a Converge song) pushed further, incorporating blackgaze and post-metal elements. Both envy and Heaven in Her Arms are cited as strong influences on Deafheaven, creating a feedback loop back to the West.

Ling Tosite Sigure, formed 2002, combined post-hardcore intensity with math rock complexity. Signed to Sony 2008. Rapid tempo changes and complex guitar melodies.

## Post-Rock

Term coined by Simon Reynolds in 1994 reviewing Bark Psychosis' Hex, defining it as "using rock instrumentation for non-rock purposes."

### Foundational Albums

Talk Talk's Spirit of Eden (1988) demonstrated a mainstream pop band could abandon song structure for jazz-influenced, orchestral-scale compositions built from silence and space. Successor Laughing Stock continued this. Used rock instrumentation as palette for texture and dynamics. Pointed toward ambient/orchestral post-rock branch.

Slint's Spiderland (1991) introduced whispered-to-screamed vocals, bass-driven grooves, dramatic dynamic shifts. Template for loud-quiet-loud post-rock. Pointed toward heavy, crescendo-driven branch. Also became DNA of math rock.

Tortoise's Millions Now Living Will Never Die (1996) consolidated post-rock as a genre. Incorporated krautrock, dub, minimal music, electronica, and jazz. David Pajo (formerly of Slint) joined, fusing krautrock complexities with dub and electronica.

### Crescendo/Cinematic Branch

Godspeed You! Black Emperor, Mogwai, Explosions in the Sky, MONO. Long-form compositions building from quiet to overwhelming volume.

Godspeed You! Black Emperor formed in Montreal 1994. Extended compositions by shifting musician collectives. Lift Your Skinny Fists Like Antennas to Heaven (2000): four tracks, two hours, field recordings, spoken word fragments. Drew from punk DIY ethic, anarchist collectives, Steve Reich, Philip Glass, Brian Eno.

MONO formed in Tokyo 1999. Most internationally recognized Japanese post-rock act. Partnership with Steve Albini began 2004, continued until Albini's death 2024. Recorded nearly all albums at Albini's Electrical Audio studio in Chicago. Albini's philosophy -- capturing live performance with minimal processing, prioritizing room sound -- suited MONO's maximalist crescendos.

### Textural/Ambient Branch

Sigur Ros formed Reykjavik 1994. Jonsi Birgisson's cello bow on guitar plus falsetto vocals in "Hopelandic" (invented language) created orchestral textures within rock format. () (2002) used only Hopelandic vocals, removing linguistic meaning entirely.

Stars of the Lid, Hammock occupy this space alongside Sigur Ros.

### Jazz-Influenced Branch

Tortoise, Do Make Say Think. Rhythmically complex, drawing from dub and minimalism.

## Shoegaze

### Origins

Originated UK, particularly London and Thames Valley, late 1980s. Term coined as insult -- reference to musicians staring at effects pedals. Roots in Jesus and Mary Chain (pop melodies with feedback walls), Cocteau Twins (ethereal vocals, shimmering guitar textures), and My Bloody Valentine.

### My Bloody Valentine

Loveless (1991) is shoegaze's defining album. Recorded across nineteen studios over two and a half years. Pioneered tremolo-arm manipulation, reversed guitar processing, layered tunings. Kevin Shields's "glide guitar" technique using whammy bar created the genre's signature shimmering, woozy textures.

MBV's Isn't Anything was specifically influenced by the distorted guitar-based noise rock of Dinosaur Jr. and Sonic Youth. Kevin Shields cited Sonic Youth as a key inspiration.

Loveless was hugely successful in Japan, helping spawn the domestic shoegaze scene.

### Other First-Wave Shoegaze

Slowdive and Ride represented Thames Valley contingent. Chapterhouse, Lush, Pale Saints filled out the first wave. Genre largely killed off by Britpop's rise mid-1990s.

### Dream Pop

Overlaps with shoegaze. "Shoegaze" preferred in UK, "dream pop" in US. Distinction is intensity: shoegaze emphasizes wall of sound and aggressive guitar; dream pop allows more room for melody, keyboards, vocal clarity. Galaxie 500, Beach House, Cocteau Twins occupy dream pop space.

## Japanese Shoegaze

### Coaltar of the Deepers

Formed 1991. Pioneered Japanese shoegaze. Merged shoegaze wall of sound with metal and hard rock. Anticipated what would later be called blackgaze. Credited as originators of Japanese shoegaze, popularized it in Tokyo's music circles.

### Kinoko Teikoku

"Mushroom Empire," formed Tokyo 2007. Most internationally visible Japanese shoegaze band. Precision of arrangement, emotional directness, J-rock vocal styles. Drew from MBV, Ride, Radiohead, Explosions in the Sky, Jeff Buckley, and Number Girl.

Long Good Bye EP peaked #56 on Oricon Albums Chart (2013). Key albums: Uzu ni Naru (2012), Eureka (2013). Band went on hiatus 2019 when bassist Taniguchi left to manage family's Buddhist temple.

## Blackgaze

Fuses black metal's tremolo-picked guitars, blast beats, and shrieked vocals with shoegaze's layered, effects-heavy atmospherics.

### Origins

Pioneered by French musician Neige around 2005 through Alcest and Amesoeurs. Le Secret EP is considered the genre's origin point. Lantlos (German, founded 2005) became key blackgaze act when Neige joined as vocalist 2010, producing .neon and Agape.

### Deafheaven

San Francisco. Sunbather (2013) was blackgaze's breakthrough -- highest-rated album of 2013 on Metacritic year-end rankings, first metal album to top those rankings. Black metal purists denounced it; indie critics embraced it. Demonstrated genre boundaries are social constructs.

Envy and Heaven in Her Arms identified as strong influences on Deafheaven. Both Japanese bands toured with them. Japanese screamo influenced American blackgaze, which in turn influenced a new generation of Japanese post-black metal.

## Noise Rock

### No Wave Origins

Emerged from collision of punk energy with New York no wave's deliberate anti-musicality. Sonic Youth and Swans both emerged from this milieu early 1980s, inspired by no wave composers Glenn Branca and Rhys Chatham.

### Sonic Youth

Incorporated melodies into droning textures of feedback and alternate tunings. Experiments with alternate tunings and prepared guitars redefined what guitar could do. Among first noise rock bands signed to major label (1990), opening door for underground acts.

### Swans

Led by Michael Gira. Built from industrial-influenced atonality and extreme volume. Early recordings: glacially slow tempos, pummeling repetition, barked authoritarian vocals. Performances caused physical illness from volume.

Children of God (1987) marked turning point -- integrated acoustic instruments, folk melodies, beauty alongside heaviness. Soft/loud contrasts and extended song structures predated what Godspeed You! Black Emperor and others would codify as post-rock.

Soundtracks for the Blind (1996): 140-minute double album synthesizing crushing heaviness, delicate beauty, industrial noise, neo-folk, drone, found sounds, spoken word, long-form composition. Arguably established the post-rock template before post-rock canonized itself.

Reformed band (2010-2016) released The Seer (2012) and To Be Kind (2014), matching or exceeding original band's power.

### Boris

Tokyo, formed 1992. Stylistically restless: albums span drone, noise rock, stoner metal, shoegaze, pop, ambient. Draws from sludge metal, drone, noise, psychedelia, minimalism.

Collaboration with Merzbow began 2002, spanning approximately eight releases. Boris records songs, Masami Akita underpins with noise. They frame noise as culturally Japanese: "This sort of consistent noise is definitely a vital part of the Japanese psyche... Noise is Japanese blues."

Absolutego (1996): single 65-minute drone piece combining Merzbow-style noise with doom metal. Flood (2000) refined drone-metal into more listenable form. Boris functions as bridge between noise rock, drone, shoegaze, stoner metal, and experimental music.

### Melt-Banana

Formed Tokyo 1992. Compressed noise rock into hyper-speed absurdist punk. Influences include Sex Pistols, Einsturzende Neubauten, Masonna, Violent Onsen Geisha, Aphex Twin, Atari Teenage Riot. Bridges noise, grindcore, pop, punk, electronic music.

## Ambient and Drone

### Brian Eno

Coined "ambient music" with Ambient 1: Music for Airports (1979). Used tape loops of varying lengths playing simultaneously, creating slowly shifting harmonic relationships. Music designed to be actively listened to or easily ignored.

Production work with David Bowie on Berlin Trilogy (Low, "Heroes", Lodger, 1977-1979) and Talking Heads on Remain in Light (1980) demonstrated ambient principles infiltrating mainstream rock. Instrumental side B's of Low and "Heroes" were essentially ambient compositions within rock albums.

### La Monte Young and Drone

La Monte Young studied with Stockhausen at Darmstadt (1959). Created works sustaining single tones for extended durations. Composition 1960 #7: "to be held for a long time" (notes B and F#, tritone).

Theatre of Eternal Music (1962-1966) featured Young, Tony Conrad (violin), John Cale (viola/bass). Hours-long performances built on sustained harmonic drones. John Cale brought drone techniques into rock with The Velvet Underground -- viola drones on "Venus in Furs" and "Heroin."

### Berlin School

Emerged early 1970s. German artists applied drone and minimalist principles to synthesizers and sequencers.

Tangerine Dream's Zeit (1972): double album of four side-long drone pieces (organ, Mellotron, VCS3 synthesizers, tape manipulation). Influenced ambient music before Eno named it.

Klaus Schulze: Timewind (1975) balanced Wagner's harmonic language with modular synthesizer exploration. Drone's electronic synthesist.

### Stars of the Lid

Formed Austin, Texas 1993. Brought drone ambient into post-rock era. The Tired Sounds of Stars of the Lid (2001): double album with strings, brass, field recordings, no rhythm. Updated Eno's ambient principles for a generation raised on shoegaze and post-rock.

And Their Refinement of the Decline (2007): triple-vinyl/double-CD. Processed orchestral sessions into compositions between contemporary classical and pure ambience.

### Hammock

Formed Nashville 2005. Both members Christians with worship music backgrounds. Ambient music with clear emotional arcs, closer to post-rock than pure ambience. Dense, beautiful, occasionally overwhelming -- instrumental shoegaze quality.

### Sunn O)))

Formed Seattle 1998. Heavily detuned guitars through massive amplifier stacks. Music as physical experience -- fog machines, robed figures, sustained notes for minutes, volumes causing nausea. Key albums: Flight of the Behemoth (2002), White1 (2003), Black One (2005). Proved heaviness and drone are natural partners through duration and sustained power.

## Windmill Scene

Coalesced around the Windmill pub in Brixton, London, late 2010s. Producer Dan Carey founded Speedy Wunderground label. Key recording rule: everything completed within a day, rarely beyond a third take. Philosophy of spontaneity shaped the scene's raw, improvisational character.

Black midi channeled Beefheart, King Crimson, and free jazz into maximalist assault. Black Country, New Road drew from klezmer, Slint, and chamber music. Squid pursued dance-oriented post-punk informed by krautrock and Afrobeat. Shame played urgent, Wire-inflected post-punk. Dry Cleaning paired deadpan spoken word with taut angular guitar pop.

# Russian and Eastern European Underground

## Leningrad Rock Club

Founded March 7, 1981, at the Inter-Union House of Amateur Art on Rubinshtein Street, Leningrad. Proposed by brothers Gennady and Vladimir Zaitsev. Organized similarly to the Union of Soviet Composers as a containment strategy — authorities used it to censor lyrics, issue permits, and monitor activity. KGB attended undercover. Despite surveillance, it became the primary meeting and performance space for Leningrad's underground rock scene.

Soviet rock spread through magnitizdat — hand-to-hand tape trading networks outside state control. Albums recorded in this format were called magnitoal'bomy.

The Red Wave compilation (Big Time Records, Australia, 1986) was the first release of Russian rock in the United States. Featured four Leningrad Rock Club bands: Aquarium, Kino, Alisa, and Strannye Igry.

## Aquarium

Formed Leningrad, 1972. Founded by Boris Grebenshchikov (mathematician) and Anatoly Gunitsky (playwright, absurdist poet). One of the founders of Russian rock. Grebenshchikov is called the "Grandfather of Russian Rock."

Early recordings explored Oriental thought and mysticism. Key albums: "The Blue Album" (1981), "Triangle" (1983) — both Russian rock classics. Approximately 21 official albums, 12 unofficial, and numerous live records.

Grebenshchikov's "Radio Silence" (1989) was produced by Dave Stewart (Eurythmics) — his biggest hit outside Russia. His trajectory moved toward spiritual and folk influences, distinct from the post-punk austerity of Kino.

## Auktyon

Formed at the Polytechnic Institute of Leningrad by Leonid Fyodorov and Oleg Garkusha. Leningrad art-rock — theatrical, improvisational, circus-like. Stable lineup achieved 1986, debut "Vernis' v Sorrento." Became an improvisational percussion-based ensemble by 1988-89. Most creative period: late 1980s to early 1990s. Concerts known for circus-like theatricality. Staple of the Leningrad Rock Club.

## Sergey Kuryokhin

Avant-garde performer and prankster from Leningrad. Led Pop-Mekhanika — a noise orchestra combining rock, classical, live animals, and performance art. As much performance artist as musician.

Famous 1991 TV appearance on Pyatoe Koleso where he fabricated a theory that Lenin consumed psychedelic mushrooms and transformed into a mushroom and a radio wave, using logical fallacies to create the illusion of reasoned argument. The stunt exploited glasnost-era trust in television and forced millions to question popular conceptions of Lenin.

Legacy: DakhaBrakha won the Sergey Kuryokhin Contemporary Art Award (2009). Shortparis follows his legacy of provocative performance art (per The Quietus). Pop-Mekhanika's noise orchestrations share DNA with chaotic experimentalism like black midi's approach.

## Kino / Viktor Tsoi

Formed Leningrad, 1982. Viktor Tsoi, vocalist. Voice of a generation through austere minimalism and deadpan delivery capturing late-Soviet existential malaise. Began performing at the Leningrad Rock Club in spring 1982; met Boris Grebenshchikov there.

Key album: "Noch" (Night, 1986) — 11 tracks of minimalistic post-punk, recorded at Andrei Tropillo's home studio. Released without the band's knowledge. Tracks include "Videli noch," "Filmy," "Mama Anarchy."

Breakthrough: the 1987 film Assa (dir. Sergei Solovyov), a cultural watershed marking when Perestroika reached mass culture. Tsoi performs "Khochu peremen" (I Want Changes) in the final scene — the song became linked with late-1980s Soviet social transformation. Gorbachev cited it as a catalyst.

"Gruppa Krovi" (Blood Type, 1988) — considered Kino's most successful album. Mature work maintaining underground spirit. Released nearly simultaneously with films Assa and Igla.

Viktor Tsoi died August 15, 1990 in a car crash in Latvia, age 28 — fell asleep driving. The Tsoi Wall at Arbat Street/Krivoarbatsky Lane, Moscow, became a pilgrimage site. The phrase "Tsoi zhiv!" (Tsoi is alive!) appeared the day of his death and spread across the USSR. Unlike the official "Lenin lives forever," it aroused genuine enthusiasm.

Kino is the most important influence on post-Soviet Cold Wave. Tsoi is dubbed Russia's Jim Morrison.

## Post-Soviet Cold Wave

Genre emerging after the 1991 Soviet collapse. Influenced by UK post-punk (The Cure, Joy Division, Depeche Mode) and most significantly by Kino. Western music flooded in after dissolution, changing the underground's dynamic.

**Daite Tank (!)** — Formed Kolomna, 2007 by Dmitry Mozzhukhin and Alexander Romankin. Name from GTA 3 cheat code ("giveusatank"). Vocalist Mozzhukhin combines singing with stream-of-consciousness narrative. Alternative rock and post-punk. Concerts in Moscow, St. Petersburg, and other cities.

**Mother Teresa (Мать Тереза)** — From Michurinsk. Darker Russian post-punk described as "Viktor Tsoi on a bad day." Albums: "Krizis zhanra" (2017), "Illyuziya" (2017), "Pozdno Domoy" (2019), "Obrazy" (2020). Earlier "Black Box Sessions (1996-98)." Distributed through Sierpien Records, a label specializing in obscure post-punk and new wave from Russia and Eastern Europe.

Late 2010s saw global resurgence of Russian and Belarusian post-punk, particularly Cold Wave. Young musicians rebuilt underground rock in Moscow and St. Petersburg, combining rock with electronic production.

## Molchat Doma

Formed Minsk, Belarus, 2017. Members: Egor Shkutko, Roman Komogortsev, Pavel Kozlov. Name means "Houses Are Silent" in Russian. Belarusian post-punk.

Debut "S Krysh Nashikh Domov" (2017), self-released, later reissued by Detriti Records (Germany). Second album "Etazhi" (2018, Detriti Records) was the breakthrough. "Sudno (Boris Ryzhy)" went viral on TikTok in early-mid 2020 (106,000+ clips). Song appeared on Spotify's Viral 50 Global. Signed to Sacred Bones Records January 2020; Etazhi reissued March 2020 (first North American pressing). Third album "Monument" (2020). Fourth album "Belaya Polosa" (2024).

Frontman Shkutko expressed concern that "the idea of the song has been lost" — "Sudno" is about poet Boris Ryzhy contemplating suicide. Aesthetic built on Soviet brutalist architecture, Cold War nostalgia, grainy VHS visuals.

Labels: Detriti Records (Germany), Sacred Bones Records (US).

## Ploho

Formed Novosibirsk, Siberia, 2013. Members: Viktor Uzhakov (vocals/guitar), Andrei Smorgonsky (bass), Igor Starshinov (keyboards). Known for balance between cold intensity and depressive sound with danceable synth-pop rhythms. Lo-fi aesthetic blending early post-punk, new wave, and Russian rock, evoking late 1980s/early 1990s atmosphere.

Albums: "Novostroiki" (2015), "Kultura dominirovaniya" (2016), "Bumazhnye bomby" (2017), "Kuda ptitsy uletayut umirat" (2018), "Pyl" (2019), "Fantomnye Chuvstva" (2021), "Kogda dusha spit" (2022), "Pochva" (2024).

Signed to Artoffact Records. Key figure alongside Molchat Doma in introducing post-Soviet post-punk to Western audiences.

## Shortparis

Formed Saint Petersburg, 2012. Avant-garde collective blending post-punk, electronic, and industrial music with theatrical performance. Founding members: vocalist Nikolai Komlev, multi-instrumentalist Aleksandr Sklyar.

Musical characteristics: dual structure balancing harmony with dissonance, irrational/spastic dance grooves, synthesizers, drum machines, slide guitar. Draws from post-punk and art-punk, infusing aggressive skinhead-era punk rhythms with electronic and industrial elements. Performs in Russian, French, and English.

Live performances resemble contemporary art installations more than concerts. Critiques post-Soviet cultural stagnation and social repression. Follows Kuryokhin's legacy of provocative performance art.

Appeared on Kremlin blacklist February 2024, barring them from state-funded events. Shifted to international touring. Symbols of artistic resistance in Putin's Russia.

## DakhaBrakha

Ukrainian quartet from Kyiv. Members: Nina Garenetska, Olena Tsybulska, Iryna Kovalenko, Marko Halanevych. Originated from Kyiv's experimental Dakh Theatre. Name means "give and take" in old Ukrainian.

Genre: "ethno-chaos" — Ukrainian folk as background, merged with cabaret, jazz, rock, hip-hop, punk, rap, soul, experimental noise. Uses traditional instruments from Russia, Australia, India, and beyond. All four members trained in folklore and ethnomusicology. Vocals founded on traditional Ukrainian chordal harmony.

Won Sergey Kuryokhin Contemporary Art Award (2009) and Shevchenko National Prize (2020). North American debut at Toronto's Luminato Festival (2013); 18 subsequent North American tours. Ukraine's best-known cultural ambassadors.

Since Russia's 2022 invasion, their tours have become platforms for anti-war messages and Ukrainian cultural identity.

## Eastern European Metal

**Second To Sun** — Russian black metal. Album "The Black" features blackened groove with clean production — heavy groove metal against grim dissonant backing. Distinct from black metal purists while retaining darkened atmosphere.

**Svrm** — Ukrainian one-man atmospheric black metal project by Serhiy Tkachenko. Previously in punk-infused pagan black metal band Zavodj (Creek) and black/crust band Life is Hell. Early demos: evocative guitar lines, bleak and raw atmospheric black metal with strong melodic tremolo work. From 2017, leaned into depressive rock-influenced sound resembling Alcest's shimmer, with melodic leads similar to Forgotten Tomb or Lustre. Spans atmospheric black metal, blackgaze, and DSBM.

**Hoplites (Ὁπλίτης)** — One-man post-black metal project from China by Liu Zhenyang, singing entirely in ancient Greek. Debuted with three albums in 2023. Mixes dissonant death metal and black metal with mathcore and avant-garde elements. "Pseudomene": 38 minutes of experimental avant-garde black metal. Liu aims to communicate authentically in ancient Greek, not just translate. As much mathcore as black metal, with hardcore punk insurgency. Uses the project to address social injustices.

**Arkona** — Russian folk/pagan metal. Founded February 2002 by Masha "Scream" Arkhipova and Alexander "Warlock" Korolyov. Lyrics draw on Russian folklore and Slavic mythology. Incorporates traditional Russian instruments. Represents pre-Christian Slavic identity distinct from Soviet/post-Soviet narratives.

## Sierpien Records

Label specializing in obscure post-punk and new wave from Russia and Eastern Europe. Distributes Mother Teresa and similar acts.

## Impact of Russia-Ukraine War on Music Scenes

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine (February 2022) fractured the post-Soviet music space. In Ukraine: explosion of music creation, shift from Russian to Ukrainian language, increased focus on Ukrainian heritage and national identity. More venues opened for Ukrainian acts as Russian bookings ceased. Audiences consciously shifted to Ukrainian music.

Kalush Orchestra's "Stefania" became a global hit after winning Eurovision 2022. Boombox frontman Andriy Khlyvnyuk performed a Ukrainian patriotic song in Kyiv's Sofiyska Square while holding a rifle — the video went viral as a resistance anthem.

In Russia: many musicians cancelled shows in protest. Those who criticized the war were blacklisted and prohibited from performing. Oxxxymiron publicly opposed the war. Spotify, Apple Music, Sound Exchange, Kobalt, Fuga, Downtown, Livenation, and others ceased Russian operations.

Shortparis blacklisted by the Kremlin (February 2024), shifted to international touring. DakhaBrakha became Ukrainian cultural ambassadors. Molchat Doma (Belarusian) occupy an ambiguous position — culturally close to both sides, but from a country whose government supported Russia's invasion.

## Cross-Scene Connections

Kino's austere minimalism echoes Joy Division. Molchat Doma and Ploho engage with Cold Wave aesthetics shared by Western post-punk revival. Kuryokhin/Auktyon's theatrical absurdism parallels Windmill scene art-rock provocations — Pop-Mekhanika's noise orchestrations share DNA with black midi's chaotic experimentalism. DakhaBrakha's ethno-chaos connects to folk-experimental hybrids. Svrm's depressive rock-influenced sound shares territory with blackgaze. Hoplites' mathcore-inflected black metal resonates with genre-blurring extremity.

# Classical Foundations

## Johann Sebastian Bach (1685-1750)

Baroque composer. Apex of Western counterpoint — multiple independent melodic lines interacting under rigorous harmonic rules. Key works: *Well-Tempered Clavier*, *The Art of Fugue*.

Bach's contrapuntal logic is foundational to jazz harmony. The Modern Jazz Quartet drew explicitly on fugal composition in the 1950s. Bill Evans studied Bach's voicings. Keith Jarrett recorded the *Well-Tempered Clavier* (1987). Brad Mehldau plays Bach transcriptions alongside original compositions.

Progressive rock applies contrapuntal thinking to rock instrumentation: early King Crimson, Gentle Giant, Frank Zappa's orchestral works. Yes's "Close to the Edge" and Gentle Giant's "The Advent of Panurge" use fugal layering of themes.

Math rock bands (Battles, Don Caballero, Hella) layer independent rhythmic and melodic lines — Bachian counterpoint with electric guitars and drums.

In metal, Opeth constructs passages where guitar, bass, and keys move in independent melodic lines. Between the Buried and Me layers polyrhythmic riffs using contrapuntal principles.

## Claude Debussy (1862-1918)

French impressionist composer. Prioritized timbre and texture over traditional harmonic progression — sound as color, harmony as atmosphere. Influenced by Japanese music heard at the 1889 Paris Exposition Universelle and by Japanese woodblock prints. Key work: *La Mer* — pentatonic scales, non-functional harmony, color over structural development.

Debussy's timbral aesthetic connects directly to shoegaze. Kevin Shields' pitch-ambiguous guitar washes operate from an impressionist sensibility. Relevant artists: My Bloody Valentine, Slowdive, Lush, Kinoko Teikoku.

Japanese composers absorbed French impressionism as an alternative to German Romantic tradition. This gave Japanese musicians a Western classical language prioritizing space, texture, and timbral color over Germanic developmental logic. Debussy's influence appears in Ryuichi Sakamoto's harmonic language, Yoko Kanno's orchestration, and in Japanese shoegaze and post-rock.

## Maurice Ravel (1875-1937)

French impressionist composer. Known for meticulous orchestration and extreme timbral detail. *Bolero* passes a single crescendoing theme through the entire orchestra — a study in how instrumental color transforms identical material.

## Igor Stravinsky (1882-1971)

Russian-born modernist composer. Marks the rupture between Romantic convention and musical modernism.

*The Rite of Spring* (1913) — caused a riot at its Paris premiere. Revolutionary elements:

**Polyrhythm and polymeter**: Different sections playing in different meters simultaneously. Directly connects to math rock (Battles, Tera Melos). The opening bassoon solo distorts folklore into angular modernism.

**Rhythm as primary element**: Pre-Stravinsky, rhythm served melody and harmony. Stravinsky made rhythm the generative principle. Connects to krautrock (CAN, Neu! — rhythm as hypnotic primary material) and to James Brown's funk innovations (breakbeat as eternal present).

**Ostinato and repetition**: Obsessive repetition of rhythmic cells flows into minimalism (Reich, Glass), then into post-rock (Godspeed You! Black Emperor, Swans, Mogwai).

**Orchestral violence**: The orchestra as brutal force. Reappears in noise rock (Swans, The Jesus Lizard, Unsane), sludge metal (Neurosis, Isis), and drone metal (Earth).

Later neoclassical period: *Pulcinella* reinterprets Baroque music through modernist ears. Prefigures film composers (Zimmer, Sakamoto) using orchestral language in modern contexts, and neoclassical work of Julia Kent.

Stravinsky's rhythmic innovations connect to progressive rock and metal: Tool uses 7/8 and 5/4 meters, King Crimson constructs polyrhythmic passages, Between the Buried and Me writes constantly shifting time signatures.

## Bela Bartok (1881-1945)

Hungarian composer. Model for integrating folk music into modernist composition. Conducted ethnomusicological field recordings of Hungarian, Romanian, and Eastern European folk music.

Bartok's method: absorbing modal language and rhythmic characteristics of folk music into original composition (not arranging folk tunes). This prefigures how metal bands integrate folk elements without becoming "folk rock" — Opeth's folk-inflected acoustic interludes follow this approach.

Rhythmic complexity in *String Quartet No. 5* and *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta* — intricate structures and asymmetrical meters that reappear in math rock and progressive metal.

Bartok's folk-integration methodology parallels Japanese composers: Sakamoto incorporating traditional Japanese instruments into electronic/orchestral contexts, Kanno drawing on Japanese folk materials in anime scores.

## Dmitri Shostakovich (1906-1975)

Soviet composer. Wrote under Stalin's totalitarian regime where artistic choices carried mortal risk.

*Symphony No. 5* — officially "a Soviet artist's response to just criticism," saved his career after his opera was condemned. The finale's triumphant D major widely interpreted as forced optimism — public triumph encoding private terror.

His 15 string quartets are his most personal music. *String Quartet No. 8*, dedicated "to the victims of fascism and war," is autobiographical — quotes his own works and uses his musical monogram D-S-C-H.

Connects to Russian rock under censorship: Kino and Aquarium operated under similar conditions of coded meaning. Viktor Tsoi used metaphor to critique Soviet society. Shared methodology of encoding meaning when direct statement is dangerous.

## Minimalism

Emerged in the 1960s as reaction against serialist complexity and Romantic emotionalism.

### Steve Reich

Key works: *Music for 18 Musicians* (1976), *Different Trains* (1988). Core techniques: phase shifting, gradual process, additive rhythm.

Reich's influence propagates into: krautrock (CAN's *Tago Mago*, Neu!'s motorik rhythms — metronomic, trance-inducing, minimalist rhythm applied to rock); post-rock (Godspeed You! Black Emperor, Mogwai, Explosions in the Sky — long-form builds from repeated cells); math rock (Battles, Tortoise — phase-shifting patterns through precise performance); ambient (Brian Eno explicitly influenced by minimalism, connecting to Stars of the Lid, Tim Hecker, Grouper).

### Philip Glass

Arpeggiated patterns and repetitive structures. *Einstein on the Beach* showed minimalism could sustain long-form drama. Film scores (*Koyaanisqatsi*) showed minimalism works in cinematic contexts.

Glass's arpeggiated piano patterns influence mouse on the keys (Glass-meets-Japan hybrid of arpeggiated piano over jazz drums).

### Terry Riley

*In C* (1964) — 53 short musical phrases that performers cycle through independently, creating ever-shifting collective texture. Pioneered the acceptance of repetition as primary material.

## Karlheinz Stockhausen (1928-2007)

German avant-garde composer. Meeting point of academic modernism and experimental electronics.

*Gesang der Junglinge* (1955-56) — one of the first works integrating human voice with electronic sounds. *Kontakte* (1958-60) — demonstrated electronic music could have physical presence and rhythmic drive.

### Stockhausen to CAN pipeline

Holger Czukay and Irmin Schmidt studied with Stockhausen in the early 1960s. When they formed CAN, they applied avant-garde techniques to rock: tape manipulation and found sounds (from musique concrete training), collective groove-based improvisation (open-form concepts into rock territory), studio as compositional instrument.

Stockhausen's broader influence: established electronic sound as legitimate compositional material. Indirect influence on Aphex Twin, Autechre, Flying Lotus. Also on Radiohead's electronic experimentation (*Kid A*), Bjork's integration of electronics into song structures.

## Ryuichi Sakamoto (1952-2023)

Japanese composer, pianist, electronic musician. Full integration of classical training, electronic experimentation, and popular music.

Studied composition at Tokyo National University of Fine Arts and Music (traditional and electronic music). Thesis on avant-garde composition.

**Yellow Magic Orchestra** (with Haruomi Hosono and Yukihiro Takahashi): Pioneering electronic pop. *Solid State Survivor* (1979) — experimental yet accessible, intellectually rigorous and fun. Influenced techno, electropop, and hip-hop production.

**Film scores**: *Merry Christmas Mr. Lawrence* (1983) — simple, melodic, blending Western and Japanese harmonic elements. *The Last Emperor* (1987) — Academy Award shared with David Byrne and Cong Su for Bernardo Bertolucci's film; integrates Chinese traditional music, Western orchestration, synthesizers. *The Revenant* (2015) — with Alva Noto, harsh atmospheric score; electronic textures processed to sound almost acoustic.

**Late solo work**: After cancer diagnosis (2014), work became more introspective. *async* (2017) and *12* (2023, final album released shortly before his death) — stripped to piano, field recordings, minimal processing.

Sakamoto bridges electronic music, film scores, avant-garde composition, Japanese music, and ambient works.

## Eduard Artemyev (b. 1937)

Soviet electronic composer. Studied with Stockhausen in the 1960s. Worked at the Moscow Experimental Studio of Electronic Music. Developed synthesizers and composition methods parallel to but isolated from Western developments — geopolitical isolation created a different electronic aesthetic, atmospheric and textural rather than pop-accessible.

**Tarkovsky collaborations**: *Solaris* (1972) — electronic textures contrasted with Bach's *Chorale Prelude in F Minor*; used the ANS synthesizer, a photoelectronic instrument unique to the USSR. *Mirror* (1975) — restrained blend of electronic sounds, classical quotations, and natural sounds. *Stalker* (1979) — minimal electronic sounds creating uncanny atmosphere; electronics as environmental wrongness.

Artemyev's atmospheric approach prefigures ambient music. Connects to territory later explored by Vangelis and Tangerine Dream, but developed without Western commercial pressure.

## Julia Kent

Cellist and composer. Uses cello processed and looped to create ambient and neoclassical compositions blurring acoustic and electronic.

Background: played cello in baroque pop band Rasputina, collaborated with Antony and the Johnsons. Solo work removes vocals — cello as texture-generating instrument.

Albums: *Delay*, *Green and Grey*, *Character*, *Asperities*. Multiple cello lines recorded and looped into dense harmonic and rhythmic textures. Combines minimalist repetition, post-rock textural approach, and classical melodic training.

Part of the broader post-classical movement alongside Max Richter and Olafur Arnalds — classical training and instruments in recorded contexts, incorporating electronics and production techniques from rock and electronic music.

## Hans Zimmer (b. 1957)

German-born film composer. Shifted film scoring from traditional orchestral to electronic-orchestral hybrids.

Method: electronic foundation with orchestral augmentation (thinks in synthesizers first, adds orchestra for weight and color). First success: *Rain Man* (1988).

**Batman trilogy** (Christopher Nolan): The Joker's theme is two notes — minimalist simplicity for iconic memorability. Massive orchestras (100+ players) plus electronics for overwhelming sonic density. Collaborative method — works with teams of additional composers and orchestrators, functioning as producer-composer.

**Inception**: Edith Piaf's "Non, je ne regrette rien" slowed down and processed into the brass-heavy main theme — musique concrete techniques at blockbuster scale.

**Dunkirk**: Uses the Shepard tone (auditory illusion of continuously ascending pitch) for relentless tension — film music as psychological manipulation.

Zimmer represents a philosophical shift: textural scoring that creates emotional states, replacing melodic themes that develop through narrative.

## Yoko Kanno (b. 1963)

Japanese anime composer. Known for extraordinary stylistic range and jazz-influenced orchestral writing.

**Cowboy Bebop** (performed by The Seatbelts): Blends big band jazz, blues, rock, and orchestral music. Opening theme "Tank!" is pure bebop. Genuine jazz composition (not "jazzy" pastiche) applied to anime scoring.

**Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex**: More orchestral and electronic, with Eastern European folk influences. **Macross Plus**: Electronic pop through the narrative device of Sharon Apple (virtual idol), alongside orchestral underscore.

Kanno's ability to write convincing jazz, rock, electronic, folk, and orchestral music within single soundtracks reflects a genre-agnostic approach where quality and intensity of craft matter more than categorical purity.

Kanno absorbed Debussy's impressionist influence (common to Japanese classical tradition) and Bartok's folk-integration methodology.

## 20. [2026-02-14]

Heaven in Her Arms emerged from Tokyo in the mid-2000s, named after a track from Converge's Jane Doe. Part of the third wave of Japanese screamo. Took envy's blueprint to darker, more metallic territory, combining post-rock, hardcore, and 1990s screamo.

## 21. [2026-02-14]

Both envy and Heaven in Her Arms are identified as strong influences on San Francisco's Deafheaven, whose Sunbather (2013) brought blackgaze to global attention.

## 22. [2026-02-14]

Asunojokei formed in 2014 in Taito ward, Tokyo. They absorbed Deafheaven's blackgaze back into Japanese context, blending post-black metal with Dir en Grey's extremity and mathy Shimokita-kei riffs. Toured Europe in 2023 supporting Boris. This completes a feedback loop: Japanese screamo influenced American blackgaze, which influenced a new generation of Japanese post-black metal.

## 23. [2026-02-14]

Ling Tosite Sigure formed in Saitama in 2002. Mathematically precise post-hardcore drawing from shoegaze and screamo. Bridges the math rock and hardcore worlds. Rapid tempo changes, complex guitar melodies. Male vocals by TK and female vocals by 345, ranging from soft singing to screams. They took Number Girl's technical complexity further into progressive territory.

## 24. [2026-02-14]

Number Girl formed in Fukuoka in August 1995 by vocalist-guitarist Shutoku Mukai. Lineup: Mukai, guitarist Hisako Tabuchi, bassist Kentaro Nakao, drummer Ahito Inazawa. Debut School Girl Bye Bye (1997). Mukai's guitar approach: alternative punk-like slicing stroke with melodic control amidst noise. Vocal style combined shaky singing, aggressive growls, spoken word, and occasional rapping. Musical roots: Pixies, Sonic Youth, Husker Du.

## 25. [2026-02-14]

Number Girl: School Girl Distortional Addict (1999): major label debut, established them as a major name in Japanese alternative rock. Sappukei (2000): produced by Dave Fridmann, who helped balance raw power with atmospheric depth. Songs like "Urban Guitar Sayonara" incorporated traditional Japanese sounds.

## 26. [2026-02-14]

Number Girl: Num-Heavymetallic (2002): incorporated dub and min'yo (traditional Japanese folk). Band dissolved September 2002. Reunion 2019; final show December 11, 2022, at Yokohama PIA Arena MM.

## 27. [2026-02-14]

Bands citing Number Girl as inspiration: tricot, Kinoko Teikoku, Ling Tosite Sigure, Asian Kung-Fu Generation, Art-School, Base Ball Bear.

## 28. [2026-02-14]

After Number Girl's breakup, Mukai formed ZAZEN BOYS with drummer Inazawa, pushing min'yo influences, dub rhythms, and avant-garde rock structures further. In 1993, Mukai started Headache Sounds, a label championing Fukuoka talent.

## 29. [2026-02-14]

MASS OF THE FERMENTING DREGS formed in Kobe in 2002, occupying similar territory of guitar-driven intensity balanced with melodic pop vocals.

## 30. [2026-02-14]

Japanese Math Rock: Math rock arrived in Japan through layered transmission lines: Japan's deep jazz history (absorbing American jazz since the 1910s) and progressive rock culture (Bi Kyo Ran, founded 1973 as a King Crimson tribute band). Ruins, Tatsuya Yoshida's duo formed in 1985, predated the naming of math rock as a genre -- primary influence was French progressive rock band Magma. Direct American connections: toe cite Ghosts and Vodka; LITE acknowledge Battles.

## 31. [2026-02-14]

Japanese Math Rock transformation: American math rock (Slint, Don Caballero, Rodan) tended toward angularity and aggression. Japanese math rock foregrounded melody, atmosphere, and melancholy.

## 32. [2026-02-14]

toe formed in Tokyo in 2000, drawing members from screamo band Dove and a band called Reach. The Book About My Idle Plot on a Vague Anxiety (2005) established their template: spiraling clean guitars, punctuative rhythm sections, space for emotion. Drummer Kashikura Takashi is especially revered. toe founded label Machu Picchu Industrias alongside mouse on the keys, Enemies, and Tangled Hair.

## 33. [2026-02-14]

tricot formed in Kyoto in 2010. Members: vocalist-guitarist Ikkyu Nakajima, guitarist Motifour, bassist Hirohiro. Self-released debut T H E (2013) brought math rock's technical finesse into pop territory. Motifour cited Number Girl, ACIDMAN, and Sheena Ringo as main influences. Based in Kyoto rather than Tokyo, they built independent pathways outside the capital's infrastructure.

## 34. [2026-02-14]

mouse on the keys: two former members of post-hardcore band Nine Days Wonder plus Daisuke Niitome from jazz-funk backgrounds. Blend minimalist neoclassical music with hard-hitting rock. Cite Philip Glass and Helmet as twin reference points. Hardcore musicians who picked up jazz and classical instruments while retaining rhythmic intensity.

## 35. [2026-02-14]

Fishmans formed in Minato, Tokyo in summer 1987 at Meiji Gakuin University. Started as a reggae band, evolved into ethereal psychedelic pop anchored by Shinji Sato's otherworldly falsetto. Discovered by Kazufumi Kodama of dub band Mute Beat. Producer ZAK added space rock, ambient, and shoegaze elements to their dub foundation.

## 36. [2026-02-14]

Fishmans magnum opus Long Season (1996): a 35-minute single track. Shinji Sato died of heart failure on March 15, 1999, at age 33.

## 37. [2026-02-14]

Fishmans' position: too experimental for city pop, too melodic for pure avant-garde. Their integration of dub production with indie songwriting anticipated trends Western artists would explore much later. Posthumous influence on artists including Mac DeMarco, Cornelius, and betcover!!.

## 38. [2026-02-14]

downy, founded by Aoki Robin in 2000, is considered a founder of Japanese post-rock. Sound blends shoegaze, post-rock, and progressive rock with irregular time signatures. All seven albums are titled Mudai ("Untitled").

## 39. [2026-02-14]

OGRE YOU ASSHOLE formed in Nagano in 2001. Influences: Talking Heads, CAN, Fugazi. Post-punk framework evolving into krautrock, shoegaze, and psychedelic territory. Band name originated when a Modest Mouse bassist, drunk after a gig, wrote "Ogre You Asshole" (a line from Revenge of the Nerds) on original drummer Arata Nishi's arm. Both downy and OGRE YOU ASSHOLE operate in Tokyo's Shimokitazawa and Koenji neighborhoods.

## 40. [2026-02-14]

Tokyo: Shimokitazawa is the spiritual center of Japanese indie rock. Key venues: Shelter (opened 1991, important for post-hardcore and indie), Basement Bar, Three (both below a liquor store and a doctor's clinic), Club Que. Multiple live houses in a small walkable area enable venue-hopping.

## 41. [2026-02-14]

Shimokita-kei movement peaked from late 1990s to mid-2000s, producing Asian Kung-Fu Generation, BUMP OF CHICKEN, Art-School. Tokyo's infrastructure supports clear pathways: live house to indie label to major label.

## 42. [2026-02-14]

Koenji, another Tokyo neighborhood, developed a parallel scene. Antiknock became a major venue for metal and punk. Shimokitazawa and Koenji form Tokyo's indie axis.

## 43. [2026-02-14]

Osaka and Kansai music venues: Fandango (founded 1987): gravitational center for free-form improvisation and experimental music. Namba Bears, established by Boredoms guitarist Seiichi Yamamoto, anchors the Namba district. Hokage specializes in heavy music. Sengoku Daitouryo is considered one of the most interesting punk houses in the world.

## 44. [2026-02-14]

Osaka's smaller scale forces cross-pollination: mixed bills are near-standard, with noise artists sharing stages with punk, psychedelic alongside hardcore.

## 45. [2026-02-14]

Kyoto sits between Osaka's chaos and Tokyo's professionalism. Les Rallizes Denudes emerged from its student radical culture. Decades later, tricot emerged from the same city with similarly uncompromising vision.

## 46. [2026-02-14]

Fukuoka: Number Girl's emergence from Fukuoka demonstrated significant Japanese bands could come from outside the Tokyo-Osaka axis. Mukai's Headache Sounds label championed Fukuoka talent.

## 47. [2026-02-14]

Live House System: Small independent venues functioning as music clubs, often doubling as bars. The quota system ("noruma") requires performing bands to sell a predetermined number of tickets. Audiences focus solely on the music -- no chatting during performances.

## 48. [2026-02-14]

Festival Infrastructure: Fuji Rock (since 1997, Naeba Ski Resort): largest outdoor festival, 200+ bands, 100,000+ attendees. Summer Sonic runs simultaneously in Osaka and Chiba. Rising Sun Rock Festival (Hokkaido): exclusively Japanese bands. Rock in Japan Festival celebrates domestic diversity. Festivals are where regional scenes intersect and become visible to national audiences.

## 49. [2026-02-14]

Happy End formed in 1969. One of the pioneering groups in establishing the legitimacy of Japanese-language rock. Lyricist Matsumoto Takashi employed techniques of double meaning and careful syllabic placement. Kaze Machi Roman (1971) proved Japanese-language rock could achieve artistic credibility and emotional authenticity.

## 50. [2026-02-14]

Happy End members: Haruomi Hosono (bass), Eiichi Ohtaki (vocals/guitar), Takashi Matsumoto (drums/lyrics), Shigeru Suzuki (guitar). All four went on to shape Japanese popular music for decades.

## 51. [2026-02-14]

After Happy End disbanded (December 31, 1972), Hosono formed Tin Pan Alley, a studio collective working in emulation of the Muscle Shoals Rhythm Section. The "Tin Pan style" -- sophisticated, groove-oriented, impeccably recorded -- laid groundwork for city pop.

## 52. [2026-02-14]

In 1978, Hosono formed Yellow Magic Orchestra (YMO) with Yukihiro Takahashi and Ryuichi Sakamoto. By 1980, YMO was the most popular group in Japan, reaching the Top 20 in American R&B charts. Pioneering use of synthesizers, samplers, sequencers, and drum machines predating much of Western electronic pop.

## 53. [2026-02-14]

Yellow Magic Orchestra: Afrika Bambaataa sampled YMO; influential on early hip-hop, techno, and electronic music. Sakamoto contrasted YMO with Kraftwerk: Kraftwerk was "theoretical, very focused, simple and minimal and strong," while YMO's approach was "very Japanese," fusing styles like a "bento box."

## 54. [2026-02-14]

YMO disbanded in 1984. Sakamoto's solo career ranged from avant-garde electronics to Oscar-winning film scores (The Last Emperor). His later ambient and neo-classical work bridged Japanese electronic innovation and global experimental music. Died 2023. In 2018, Light in the Attic Records released five Hosono albums internationally for the first time, coinciding with the global city pop revival.

## 55. [2026-02-14]

City pop emerged in the late 1970s. Characteristics: meticulous production influenced by American soft rock, AOR, jazz fusion, funk, and disco; lyrics depicting urban life and romance; heavy use of session musicians, many from the Tin Pan Alley circle.

## 56. [2026-02-14]

Tatsuro Yamashita: arguably city pop's most important figure. Ride on Time (1980) topped charts. For You (1982) further solidified his reputation. His wife Mariya Takeuchi's "Plastic Love" (1984) became the catalyst for the genre's 21st-century global revival.

## 57. [2026-02-14]

Eiichi Ohtaki's A Long Vacation (1981) sold over a million copies, won Best Album at the Japan Record Awards. All three former Happy End bandmates contributed. Taeko Onuki, a former member of Sugar Babe alongside Yamashita, developed a European-influenced aesthetic through her "European trilogy." Other golden age figures: Toshiki Kadomatsu, Anri, Miki Matsubara, Makoto Matsushita.

## 58. [2026-02-14]

City pop's golden age coincided with Japan's asset price bubble (1986-1991). Artist Hiroshi Nagai's airbrushed cover art -- sunsets, beaches, luxury cars -- became synonymous with the genre.

## 59. [2026-02-14]

City Pop Internet Revival: In 2017-2018, an eight-minute remix of Takeuchi's "Plastic Love" went viral through YouTube's recommendation algorithm. The revival was primed by vaporwave (emerging around 2010), a microgenre that used slowed-down, chopped city pop samples. Streaming platforms accelerated the spread; out-of-print records commanded hundreds on Discogs.

## 60. [2026-02-14]

Shibuya-kei flourished in mid-to-late 1990s Tokyo, named after the Shibuya district and specifically the HMV Shibuya record store, whose eclectic import selection (French ye-ye, British indie pop, bossa nova, jazz, lounge) directly informed the movement. Distinguished by a cut-and-paste approach drawing from 1960s pop, bossa nova, lounge, and jazz. Playful, ironic, self-consciously retro.

## 61. [2026-02-14]

Flipper's Guitar, the duo of Kenji Ozawa and Keigo Oyamada, laid the foundation for Shibuya-kei. Oyamada's solo project Cornelius produced Fantasma (1997), widely considered a masterpiece of 1990s Japanese pop. Pizzicato Five was the most prominent shibuya-kei act internationally, fusing J-pop with jazz, soul, and lounge.

## 62. [2026-02-14]

Modern Inheritors of City Pop and Shibuya-kei: cero: Obscure Ride (2015) updated city pop's harmonic sophistication without sounding retro. mitsume: dreamy, reverb-soaked indie pop with shibuya-kei sensibility. mei ehara: synthesizes Japanese folk, indie rock, and ambient textures. Lamp (formed 2000): domestic city pop revival predating the international internet-driven one by over a decade.

## 63. [2026-02-14]

Japanese Jazz Origins: Jazz arrived in Japan during the early 20th century through maritime influences, with groups playing on Pacific cruise ships from 1913 onward. Classified as "enemy music" during WWII but the ban didn't hold. Post-war, US military bases served as crucibles for musicians.

## 64. [2026-02-14]

Jazz kissa (listening bar): first one, Black Bird, opened in Tokyo in 1929. Born of economic necessity -- imported records were expensive, apartment walls thin. Cafes let enthusiasts hear albums on high-end equipment at conversation-killing volume. Some banned conversation entirely. Around 600 jazz kissa operated across Japan at peak. In the 21st century, at least 50 establishments inspired by jazz kissa have opened internationally.

## 65. [2026-02-14]

The 1970s is the golden age of Japanese jazz. Japanese musicians began adding "national flavor" and experimenting with free jazz, fusion funk, and bebop.

## 66. [2026-02-14]

Three Blind Mice (founded by Takeshi Fujii, 1970): known as the Japanese Blue Note, renowned for audiophile recording quality under engineer Yoshihiko Kannari. East Wind (established 1974): promoted the Japanese jazz scene and fusion recordings. DIW Records (founded 1983, subsidiary of Disk Union): documented the New York avant-garde and bridged Japanese and American scenes through approximately 300 releases including Art Ensemble of Chicago, David Murray, and John Zorn.

## 67. [2026-02-14]

Ryo Fukui: self-taught pianist in Sapporo. Scenery (1976), recorded at Yamaha Hall in Sapporo, found a growing audience through internet discovery decades later. In 1995, Fukui opened the Slowboat jazz club in Sapporo, which remains operational under his widow's management after his death in 2016.

## 68. [2026-02-14]

Masabumi Kikuchi (1939-2015). Poo-Sun (1970) channeled Miles Davis's electric period. Moved to New York in the late 1970s, worked with Sonny Rollins, Miles Davis, and McCoy Tyner. Trio with Gary Peacock and Paul Motian as Tethered Moon released albums for Winter & Winter. Late ECM recordings span post-bop, avant-garde, and modern free music.

## 69. [2026-02-14]

Hiromi Uehara (born 1979, Hamamatsu). Met Chick Corea by chance in Tokyo at age 17; he invited her to play at his concert the next day. Attended Berklee, signed before graduation. Grammy winner. Blends stride, post-bop, progressive rock, and fusion. Performed at the opening ceremony of the 2020 Tokyo Olympics.

## 70. [2026-02-14]

Other Key Japanese Jazz Figures: Hiroshi Suzuki: Cat (1975) went unnoticed on release but became one of the most sought-after Japanese jazz albums. Masaru Imada: Green Caterpillar (1975) on Three Blind Mice. Yasuaki Shimizu: pushed from jazz into ambient and Japanese folk; cited as an influence by Oneohtrix Point Never.

## 71. [2026-02-14]

Nujabes (Jun Seba, 1974-2010), Tokyo producer. Bridged jazz and hip-hop through warm analog sampling and live instrumentation. Soundtrack for Samurai Champloo brought jazz-inflected beats to a global audience. Posthumous influence on lo-fi hip-hop movement. Record stores in Shibuya specialized in underground hip-hop. His use of Japanese jazz samples introduced Western audiences to 1970s Japanese recordings, creating a feedback loop back to source material.

## 72. [2026-02-14]

Sheena Ringo debuted in 1998. Genre-defying approach combining rock, jazz, chanson, and traditional Japanese music with literary lyrics. Distinctive vibrato, retro fashion, auteur-level control. In 2004 she formed Tokyo Incidents (Tokyo Jihen), a collaborative ensemble crossing alternative rock, funk, jazz, classical, and Brazilian music. Members other than Ringo composed songs (music-first, Ringo writing lyrics afterward). Sheena Ringo paved the way for female artists to have complete creative control in Japanese pop and inspired genre-agnostic experimentation.

## 73. [2026-02-14]

KIRINJI formed in 1996 by siblings Takaki and Yasuyuki Horigome. Combined city pop sensibilities with jazz harmony and bossa nova rhythms. After Yasuyuki's departure in 2013, Takaki reformed the project with a new lineup shifting toward jazz and electronic elements.

## 74. [2026-02-14]

Hatsune Miku released August 31, 2007 by Crypton Future Media -- the first Japanese Vocaloid using the Vocaloid 2 engine. Name means "the first sound of the future." Vocaloid software democratized vocal performance, freeing producers from needing a singer.

## 75. [2026-02-14]

The Vocaloid ecosystem developed on Niconico Douga (launched December 2006), Japan's largest video-sharing service. Distinguishing feature: scrolling comments appearing directly on videos. The "-P" naming convention (producer names followed by P) became a hallmark, originally an honor bestowed by listeners.

## 76. [2026-02-14]

Kenshi Yonezu started in 2009 uploading as Hachi using Hatsune Miku. "Matryoshka" (2011) exceeded 50 million YouTube views. Transitioned to singing himself in 2012; became arguably Japan's biggest pop star.

## 77. [2026-02-14]

Eve entered through Niconico cover singing, became a Vocaloid producer. "Kaikai Kitan" (first opening theme for Jujutsu Kaisen) became the most-played Japanese song overseas on Spotify in 2021.

## 78. [2026-02-14]

YOASOBI: duo of producer Ayase and vocalist Ikura. "Novel into music" concept -- adapting written stories into songs. "Idol" (opening theme for Oshi no Ko) became the first Japanese song to top the Billboard Global Excl. US chart in 2023.

## 79. [2026-02-14]

ZUTOMAYO ("I Wish It Was Midnight All the Time"): maintained anonymity -- vocalist ACAne has never revealed her face; concerts performed behind translucent screens.

## 80. [2026-02-14]

inabakumori: Vocaloid producer. "Lagtrain" (2020) entered Niconico's Hall of Legend.

## 81. [2026-02-14]

Anime Music Pipeline: Symbiotic relationship between anime and J-pop: anime production committees often include record labels, making theme songs valuable promotional opportunities. Yoko Kanno's Cowboy Bebop soundtrack (1998), performed by her band Seatbelts, established that anime soundtracks could be artistically serious. Jazz-focused but spanning blues, bossa nova, funk, hip-hop, and hard rock. Kanno represents the artistic legitimacy of anime music for the current generation of composers.

## 82. [2026-02-14]

betcover!! is the project of Yanase Jiro, born 1999 in Chofu, Tokyo. Started in 2015 as a misspelled solo bedroom project ("betcover" from "bedcover"). Grew into a six-piece art rock ensemble fusing jazz-rock, progressive composition, dub, enka, and post-punk.

## 83. [2026-02-14]

betcover!!: After an early major-label deal with Avex's cutting edge imprint, Yanase left the system entirely. Independent albums: Jikan (2021), Tamago (2022), Uma (2023), Yuki (2025). Tamago was recorded live over two days at a Ginza studio previously used by Happy End and Ryuichi Sakamoto, without metronomes; Yanase deliberately skipped mastering to retain dynamic range.

## 84. [2026-02-14]

betcover!! album Yuki: Jim O'Rourke served as recording and mastering engineer. Saxophonist Kei Matsumaru (Berklee-trained, Apple Vinegar Music Award Grand Prize winner) brought a free jazz dimension.

## 85. [2026-02-14]

betcover!! influences: Most direct ancestor: Fishmans -- Yanase is an explicit fan, and the dub-influenced atmospheric qualities trace directly to Shinji Sato's band. Other cited influences: Jack White, Nick Cave, The Mars Volta, Dirty Three, King Tubby, Augustus Pablo, Serge Gainsbourg.

## 86. [2026-02-14]

betcover!! Connection to the UK Windmill scene: Black Country, New Road announced betcover!! as special guests for their Japan Tour 2025, following the Japan-to-UK pipeline connecting Dos Monos to black midi. BCNR expressed interest in supporting betcover!! on tour (peer-level respect). 2025 world tour: 31 cities across China, UK/Europe, and Japan.

## 87. [2026-02-14]

Bridge Figures in Japanese Music: Boris bridges noise, drone, shoegaze, stoner metal, and ambient. envy connects post-hardcore, screamo, and post-rock. Hosono threads from psychedelic rock through city pop to electronic and ambient. Nujabes links jazz to hip-hop and lo-fi culture. betcover!! synthesizes jazz-rock, dub, enka, and prog into contemporary art rock. Jim O'Rourke (American, based in Japan since the mid-2000s) connects the Japanese experimental scene to international networks through work with Merzbow, Eiko Ishibashi, Kahimi Karie, and betcover!!. Kei Matsumaru bridges Tokyo's jazz scene, the Dos Monos/black midi pipeline, and betcover!!.

## 88. [2026-02-14]

Key Connections Across the Japanese Music Ecosystem: Kinoko Teikoku wrote a song titled "Girl Meets NUMBER GIRL." Tricot's Motifour cited Number Girl alongside ACIDMAN and Sheena Ringo. Fishmans' tribute project included Cornelius and Ryuichi Sakamoto. Asunojokei toured Europe supporting Boris. The Dos Monos-to-black midi pipeline connects to betcover!! and BCNR. Coaltar of the Deepers and Kinoko Teikoku represent Japanese shoegaze influenced by Number Girl and domestic post-hardcore.

## 89. [2026-02-14]

Japanese music transformation patterns: American math rock's abrasion became melody, precision, and melancholy (toe, tricot). Japanese post-hardcore gained post-rock's cinematic dynamics (envy). Japanese noise became culturally encoded as "Japanese blues" (Boris). Japanese jazz developed kissa-driven listening culture prioritizing audiophile recording quality (Three Blind Mice, Ryo Fukui). Japanese city pop absorbed American AOR, funk, and disco, recontextualized within the economic bubble era (Yamashita, Takeuchi).

## 90. [2026-02-14]

The Dakou Generation: In the early 1990s, Western record companies shipped millions of unsold CDs to China as recyclable waste. These "dakou" (cut) discs had edges sawed to prevent resale but remained playable except the last track. Beijing's Xinjiekou South Street became the underground distribution hub where Nirvana, Sonic Youth, Joy Division, and the Velvet Underground entered China through customs as recycling materials.

## 91. [2026-02-14]

The dakou generation -- born roughly 1970-1990 -- built their aesthetic education across punk, metal, hip-hop, folk-rock, and experimental music from damaged imports. The scene's relationship to Western influence was one of salvage and recontextualization, not imitation.

## 92. [2026-02-14]

Cui Jian: Known as the "Godfather of Chinese Rock." Influences: Bob Dylan, the Beatles, the Rolling Stones, Talking Heads. His song "Nothing to My Name" (yi wu suo you), first performed May 9, 1986, mixed traditional Chinese styles with rock instrumentation. It became an unofficial anthem during the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests and made Cui Jian a cult figure among urban youth.

## 93. [2026-02-14]

Dou Wei rose to prominence as lead vocalist and songwriter of Black Panther, popularizing heavy metal and hard rock in mainland China. Left Black Panther in 1991, formed Zuomeng (The Dreaming). Solo albums: Black Dream (1994), Sunny Days (1995), Mountain River (1998).

## 94. [2026-02-14]

Dou Wei formed E band in 1999, releasing Hallucinations -- featuring ambient guitar work reminiscent of Durutti Column. Became increasingly ambient and experimental through the 2000s, exploring traditional Chinese ambient folk, experimental electronic, free jazz, and spoken word. Multi-instrumentalist: electric guitars, synthesizers, guqin zither, dizi flute, ruan lute, drums. Trajectory from stadium rock star to reclusive experimental composer established a template for artistic integrity in Chinese rock.

## 95. [2026-02-14]

Tang Dynasty and Black Panther: Late 1980s mainstream hard rock acts. Crucial to the Chinese rock scene's early infrastructure.

## 96. [2026-02-14]

Modern Sky Records founded 1997 by Shen Lihui (lead vocalist of the band Sober) to release his own album. Early signings: New Pants, Supermarket (associated with the "New Sound Movement"). Grew into China's largest independent label: 300+ artists, offices in Beijing, New York, London, Berlin. Key roster: Hedgehog, Wutiaoren, New Pants.

## 97. [2026-02-14]

Modern Sky Records functions as both commercial enterprise and cultural institution (recording studios, distribution, PR, tour booking). Launched the Strawberry Music Festival in Beijing in 2010, held in 29 Chinese cities.

## 98. [2026-02-14]

Midi Music Festival: Born at the Midi School of Music (China's first contemporary music school) in Beijing. Predates Strawberry. Harder, more underground-focused programming. Established the template for large-scale outdoor rock festivals in China.

## 99. [2026-02-14]

Other Chinese Labels: Maybe Mars released Carsick Cars' early work. Tenzenmen and various DIY operations sustained the underground with smaller budgets but greater curatorial focus.

## 100. [2026-02-14]

D-22: Venue in Beijing's Wudaokou district, founded by Michael Pettis in 2005-06. The Wall Street Journal called it "the center of new music in Beijing." Launchpad for post-punk and avant-garde musicians. Key bands: Carsick Cars, Snapline, Hedgehog, The Gar. Scene characterized by international awareness -- musicians worshipped 1980s New York no-wave, absorbed Velvet Underground and Sonic Youth, studied Steve Reich and Glenn Branca. Closed January 2012.

## 101. [2026-02-14]

School Bar: Co-founded by Liu Hao (bassist for Joyside) and Liu "Felix" Fei. Located in Beijing's Gulou hutong area. Opened 2010 as D-22 shuttered, carrying forward the scene.

## 102. [2026-02-14]

Carsick Cars formed March 2005 in Beijing by Shou Wang (Beijing Institute of Technology student), Li Weisi, and Li Qing. Debut CD produced by P.K.14 frontman Yang Haisong. Combined noise textures with hard open chords. Track "Zhong Nan Hai" cited as the anthem of Beijing's underground. Influences: no-wave. Signed to Maybe Mars.

## 103. [2026-02-14]

Re-TROS (Rebuilding the Rights of Statues) formed Beijing 2003. Sound: dark minimalism, motorik rhythms, deadpan vocals. Influences: Joy Division, Bauhaus. Album Before the Applause (2017), produced by Hector Castillo, blurred post-punk and electronica. Opened European arena dates for Depeche Mode on the Global Spirit Tour 2017.

## 104. [2026-02-14]

Snapline: Side project of Carsick Cars members Li Qing and Li Weisi. Compared to The Fall for deranged, dissonant melodies and deadpan delivery. The abrasive, art-damaged end of Beijing's post-punk spectrum.

## 105. [2026-02-14]

Nova Heart: Beijing electronic rock. Sound: nocturnal dance tracks mixing indie dance with dark psychedelic rock. Compared to Blondie and italo disco revival. Ethereal vocals, explosive live performances. Proved Beijing's experimental instincts could accommodate pop sensibilities.

## 106. [2026-02-14]

FAZI formed 2010 in Xi'an, connected to Beijing's scene. Punchy post-punk. Influences: The Cure, Joy Division. Bridges punk energy and post-punk architecture.

## 107. [2026-02-14]

New Pants (Xin Kuzi) established 1996 under Modern Sky. Iconic Chinese new wave band spanning synthpop, punk rock, alternative rock. Early influences: new wave, old-school punk. Album Dragon Tiger Human Cinnabar marked a shift toward 1980s synth-pop with nostalgic oriental elements. Retro-futurism and kitsch aesthetic. Won The Summer of Bands competition in 2019.

## 108. [2026-02-14]

Joyside formed 2001 in Beijing. Punk rock. Versatile: romantic songs ("Dong Dong Dong") and aggressive tracks ("I Wanna Piss Around You"). Dissolved 2009, returned a decade later. Subject of Kevin Fritz documentary Wasted Orient. Bassist Liu Hao co-founded School Bar.

## 109. [2026-02-14]

Brain Failure (Naozhuo) formed in high school 1997 in Beijing. Street punk. One of few Beijing punk bands to break out. Emerged alongside pioneer punk bands 69, Reflector, and Anarchy Boys.

## 110. [2026-02-14]

Underground Baby (Dixia Ying'er) created 1991 in Beijing by brothers Gao Wei and Gao Yang in their parents' cellar. Song "All the Same" (dou yi yang) considered the first Beijing punk song. Album Awakening (1999). Cult act.

## 111. [2026-02-14]

Other Beijing Underground Acts: Tongue (She Tou) and Wood Pushing Melon (Mu Tui Gua) appeared on Beijing Band 2001: New Rock Bands From The People's Republic Of China. Deep underground: Induced Society, Lonely Liri, Reversing into Garage, Bacteria Band, Cement Spell Band, Tiaoshangyang, Twisted Machine, Fly -- performed at School Bar and basement spaces.

## 112. [2026-02-14]

Hedgehog (Ciwei) began as "Out of Control" in early 2003. Shilu joined late 2004, renamed to Hedgehog. Boxuan joined as bassist October 2005, completing classic lineup with Zijian. Influences: The Jesus and Mary Chain, The Ramones, Nirvana. Signed Modern Sky 2007.

## 113. [2026-02-14]

Hedgehog albums: Noise Attack World (debut), Blue Daydream (2009), Sweet and Kill, Sun Fun Gun (2011-2012), A Newborn White Immortal (2020), Crow Valley - It's All Connected (2022). Evolved from garage punk toward expansive art rock. Breakthrough on The Summer of Bands (2019), winning CMA "Band of the Year."

## 114. [2026-02-14]

Second Hand Rose (Er Shou Mei Gui) formed Beijing 1999 by Liang Long. Combines traditional Chinese instruments with rock. Known for exaggerated stage design, rich visual effects, humorous lyrics. Vocalist Liang Long dresses as a woman on stage -- transgressive in Chinese context. Fusion of errenzhuan (Northeast Chinese folk performance) with hard rock. Performed at Swiss Snow Mountain Music Festival (2002), China-Japan Pop Music Exchange Concerts (2003), Amsterdam China Festival (2004).

## 115. [2026-02-14]

Miserable Faith (Tongku de Xinyang): Four-piece from Beijing, formed 1999. Evolved from rap-inflected metal and hardcore toward indie rock with folksy elements. Current sound spans ballad folk, pop, indie rock with traditional Chinese touches. Runs in similar vein to GALA, Omnipotent Youth Society (Wanneng Qingnian Lüdian), and Sound Fragment (Shengyin Suipian).

## 116. [2026-02-14]

Experimental Outliers in Chinese Underground: Sound Fragment, Berlin Psycho Nurses (Bolin Hushi), and Mad Doctor (Fengyi) occupy the intersection of post-punk, noise rock, and experimental electronics. Qunxiang, 8Immortals, and Non-Human Species pushed toward post-rock mixed with harsh electronics and industrial.

## 117. [2026-02-14]

Wang Wen formed 1999 in Dalian, northern China. China's most important post-rock act. Predominantly instrumental: elaborate sonic fabrics, ethereal melodies, complex passages, tempo changes, dynamic accelerations. Sound reflects gray, cold atmospheres of Dalian. Echoes of Sigur Ros and Tortoise. Known for melancholic instrumental music and intense live performances. Extensive international touring -- China's post-rock ambassadors to Europe and North America.

## 118. [2026-02-14]

Zhaoze: Southern China's key post-rock act. First post-rock band to incorporate traditional Chinese instruments. Known for "Electric Guqin" -- combining ancient Chinese guqin music with modern post-rock. Album Cang Lang Xing (Azure Star, 2017) is an in-depth rearrangement of ancient guqin music with post-rock. Album 1911 further explored this synthesis. Less concerned with crescendo dynamics, more interested in Chinese aesthetics of restraint, space, and philosophical contemplation.

## 119. [2026-02-14]

Dialect Rock: Bands refusing Mandarin in favor of regional languages, asserting local identity against Mandarin homogenization.

## 120. [2026-02-14]

Wutiaoren (Five People): Two members: Ah Mau and Yan Fo, both from Haifeng, Guangdong. Sing entirely in Haifeng dialect (a sub-dialect of Teochew, the least spoken of Guangdong's three major dialects alongside Cantonese and Hakka). Sound: folky guitar and accordion with occasional harmonica and Jew's harp. Yan Fo plays accordion -- rare in Chinese folk music, giving a distinctive Eastern European folk-rock flavor.

## 121. [2026-02-14]

Wutiaoren: Album A Tale of Haifeng documents their hometown. Signed Modern Sky 2014. Seven full-length albums. Proved dialect rock could achieve national appeal via The Summer of Bands.

## 122. [2026-02-14]

Jiulian Zhenren (Nine Connected Real People) named after a mountain in Lianping county, Guangdong. Sing exclusively in Hakka dialect. Original compositions combining Western rock with hometown sounds -- not based on traditional Hakka folk songs. Members: Ouyang Haopeng/Along (vocals, guitar), Mai Haipeng/Amai (trumpet, vocals), Ye Wanli/Wanli (bass), Li Lei/Chuimi (drums).
